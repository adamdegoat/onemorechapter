Sound Check, Kingarth

A burned-out London music producer hiding in a Scottish village gets volunteered to save the local ceilidh — and finds something he thought was broken still works.

Published 7:22 AM SGT·~14 min read

❀

Three things about Kingarth that any local will tell you without being asked: the seals on the south rocks are better than any you'll find on Bute; the chips from the van by the harbour come wrapped in paper and the paper is always slightly wet, which is somehow perfect; and the summer ceilidh, running forty-two years without interruption, will happen on the third Saturday of June if it kills everyone involved.

That last one is looking ambitious this year.

I hear about it on a Tuesday, because in Kingarth you hear about everything on a Tuesday. The post office on Tuesdays has a particular acoustic quality — something about the combination of Mrs. Paterson sorting parcels and three or four people with nowhere urgent to be — that causes information to travel efficiently. I'm there to collect a package that has been sitting behind the counter for four days because I kept forgetting to come in, which is a thing that happens to you when your entire schedule is: make coffee, walk the coastal path until your knees hurt, eat whatever requires the fewest steps, and read until it's reasonable to sleep.

The package is a set of replacement headphone ear pads. I ordered them six weeks ago and then wondered who I thought I was.

"Your parcel's here since Friday," says Mrs. Paterson, whose name is not actually Mrs. Paterson but who looks so precisely like a Mrs. Paterson that I have never successfully learned another name. She's seventy-something, sharp-eyed, wearing a hand-knitted jumper that appears to have small Highland cattle on it.

"Thank you," I say. "Sorry."

"Not at all." She sets it on the counter, then adds, as if this is a continuation of the same thought: "Terrible business about the Reel."

I am already reaching for the package. "Mm."

"Forty-two years," she says. "Never missed a one."

"Right."

"Séamus and Callum." She shakes her head with a precision that suggests she is doing far more editorial work than those two names ought to carry. "You'll have heard."

"I haven't, actually."

She looks at me with the expression of someone confirming a long-held theory. "Och, well. They've fallen out. Band's finished, apparently." She says apparently the way a prosecuting barrister says allegedly. "Which means the ceilidh's got no band, which means the ceilidh's — " She stops. Looks at me. Looks at the package. "Is that music equipment?"

"Ear pads," I say. "For headphones. Very low stakes."

"You're in music, though. Tom Kilbride said."

Tom Kilbride runs the fishing charter boats and has spoken to me exactly twice, both times about parking. I have no idea when I became a topic of conversation in the fishing charter community, but here we are. A year in Kingarth and I am apparently being quietly documented.

"I was," I say. "In London. Different kind of music."

Mrs. Paterson nods the nod of someone who considers this a temporary distinction.

The shape of my life before Kingarth: studio time starting at ten at night, ending when the sun was coming in under the door. Tracking sessions, mixing sessions, the particular psychic weight of trying to make someone's creative vision more itself while also making it commercially legible. I was good at it. I had a methodology, which is what you have when you've been doing something long enough that you can explain it to people who want to learn, which is when you know the fun is partially over. I had a studio in Hackney. I had a one-bedroom I was never in. I had a reputation for being difficult, which in the music industry means you have a reputation for being right.

Then my dad had his cardiac event — everyone in my family refers to it as the cardiac event, which suggests a degree of brand management I find reassuring — and I came to Kingarth, where he and my mum retired four years ago to the bafflement of everyone who knew them. He has recovered fully. The consultant says he is, in her words, annoyingly well for a man his age. My mother, whose coping strategy is extreme local integration, is now on the village hall committee and goes wild swimming with three women called Fiona.

I stayed. I'm not sure what the present tense of overstayed one's welcome is, but I may be living it.

The music equipment is in the second bedroom. The headphones I ordered the ear pads for are part of a monitoring chain I assembled and then never used. I walk past the closed door every day. It's fine. I'm not ready. I'm not ready, and Kingarth does not particularly care whether I'm ready, which is almost the thing I like best about it.

The pub is called The Anchor, which is either extremely appropriate or the least imaginative name in Scotland. Angus, who runs it, is built like a man who has been specifically designed to run a pub in a small Scottish village: wide, calm, with a face that would be frightening in another context but here just means you feel safe. He makes a point of knowing what everybody drinks. He put a half-pint of Tennent's in front of me the second week I was here, when I hadn't ordered, and I drank it without comment because it seemed like the right move.

Thursday evening. I'm in my usual spot, which is the chair in the corner that doesn't quite face the door, which is how I communicate that I am here but not fully present. There are four other people in the pub. The smell is peat smoke and old wood and something yeasty and warm that I have stopped trying to name.

Angus slides a pint — a full pint, not my usual half — onto the table without being asked.

I look up.

He says nothing. He walks back to the bar. There is an extremely long pause during which I stare at the pint and Angus pretends to do something with a cloth.

Then he says, still to the cloth: "It's a shame about the ceilidh."

"Everyone keeps telling me that," I say.

"Right enough."

Pause.

"Séamus played at my wedding," he says. "Both of them did. Nineteen ninety-six. My wife still talks about it."

I don't say anything.

"You'd be welcome here for the night of it, of course. There'll still be a gathering. Just — " He trails off. Does a slight thing with his shoulders. Goes back to the cloth.

The pint, I note, is exactly the right temperature.

The thing about The Kingarth Reel is — and I am only learning this through the general osmosis of the village — they are genuinely good. Not talented-for-a-village-band good. Actually good. Séamus Brennan plays fiddle the way some people breathe: with complete unconscious fluency. Callum Doak on accordion is a different thing, more deliberate, knowing every note, but when they're together they reportedly have this ease, this particular rightness, that people drive from the mainland to hear.

Had. Past tense.

What happened, as far as I can tell from the various secondhand accounts: there was a rehearsal, there were words, Callum walked out, Séamus hasn't returned calls, the rest of the Reel — two younger players who joined about eight years ago — are in an impossible position. The ceilidh committee, which my mother sits on but is professionally staying out of because she is correctly anticipating that I will be annoyed at her for things she hasn't done yet, has made approximately fifteen attempts to intervene and achieved nothing.

Twelve days.

Eilidh Macrae is sixteen, my neighbour's daughter, and has apparently been running a parallel investigative operation without my knowledge.

She appears at my door on a Friday morning while I'm on my second coffee, holding a spiral-bound notebook and looking like someone who has completed a task and wants credit for it. She has paint on her hands — she's an artist, I've gathered, serious about it — and the notebook is covered in small drawings of animals in the margins.

"I've got a list," she says, by way of greeting.

"Of what?"

"Musicians." She opens the notebook. "There's Priya Gupta, she's at Rothesay, she plays guitar and mandolin. Donald Gillies from Port Bannatyne plays bouzouki — I know it sounds wrong but he's actually good, he does sessions. Morag — that's Morag Sinclair, she's nearby — she plays piano and also does percussion. And there's my cousin Rab, who plays fiddle, he's not Séamus, obviously, but — "

"Eilidh."

She stops.

"Why are you here?" I ask. "Specifically at my door."

She looks at me with the impatience of someone who thought this part was obvious. "Because you know how to put a band together."

"I make records."

"That's the same thing."

"It really isn't."

She holds up the notebook. The list has phone numbers. It has, I notice, availability notes — who can do which dates, who has transport, who would need accommodation. Someone has put a small star next to the names she considers essential.

"Did you make this yourself?" I ask.

"Yes."

"Have you called any of these people?"

"I was waiting," she says, "until I had a producer."

She says producer in a voice that is entirely straight-faced. I want to tell her she's been underestimating the number of times that word has been used on me in a manipulative context, but I'm also aware that she's sixteen and drew an otter in the margin of her musicians list and has done, objectively, a lot of legwork.

"I'm not your producer," I say.

"Okay," she says, and doesn't move.

I drink my coffee.

"Leave the notebook," I say, finally.

She leaves the notebook. She takes the stairs off my porch at a run.

There is something I haven't told Eilidh, or Angus, or Mrs. Paterson, which is that I know exactly why I haven't touched my equipment. It's not grief, it's not burnout, it's not the cardiac event, though all of those contributed to the general climate. It's that the last project I worked on — four months before I came here — was an album that wasn't finished when the artist and I had a falling out that was professionally public and personally worse. It went wrong in several directions simultaneously. The album exists, half-mixed, in a hard drive in the second bedroom along with the monitoring chain and the ear pads I have now successfully retrieved. I don't know what to do with it. Not yet.

I tell myself this is about Kingarth being restorative. I tell myself I'm taking time. But the more honest version is that I'm scared that if I sit down in front of a mix again, I will discover that I no longer know how to do it. And that would be definitive in a way that fourteen months of avoidance isn't.

I look at Eilidh's notebook for a long time.

Séamus Brennan lives in a stone cottage at the north end of the village. I walk there on Saturday morning because it seems better than calling. He answers the door in a wool jumper and the expression of a man who has recently had many visitors he didn't want.

"I'm not interested," he says, before I've spoken.

"I haven't said anything."

"You've got that notebook. I've seen it going around."

"I'm here to talk," I say, "not to recruit. I don't actually care that much about the ceilidh."

That stops him. He looks at me with sudden attention, as if I've done something unexpected. He opens the door wider. We end up at his kitchen table with tea I didn't ask for, a view of the water through a small window, and the silence of a man who has lived alone long enough to make quiet feel deliberate.

The fiddle is on the table. Which is a strange place for a fiddle when you're not playing.

"How long have you and Callum been playing together?" I ask.

"Thirty years," he says. "Near enough."

"And the falling out — "

"That's my business."

"Sure," I say.

Another silence. The water outside is grey-green and flat. There's a gannet doing something elaborate above the rocks.

"It's about his daughter," Séamus says, eventually, and then stops.

I wait.

"Fiona." He says it carefully. "She died. Eighteen months ago. She was thirty-one." He looks at the table. "Callum's not — he hasn't been right. Anyone could see it. I tried to talk to him, at rehearsal. Said I was worried about him. Said — " He stops again. His jaw does something difficult. "I said he didn't have to keep coming if he wasn't up to it."

He says this with the flatness of a man who has replayed it many times.

"And he heard that differently than you meant it," I say.

Séamus looks up. "He heard me saying he was useless. That's what he's told the others. That I said he was no good anymore and should step aside." His voice is measured and extremely controlled. "I meant the opposite. I meant I could see him struggling and I didn't want to add to it."

We sit with this.

"Have you told him what you just told me?" I ask.

"I've tried to call."

"He's not picking up?"

"He's picking up," Séamus says. "He's just not — " He makes a slight gesture that encompasses, I think, the entire difficulty of being a man in his sixties who was raised not to have this conversation. "It's not an easy thing to say."

"No," I agree.

"I don't know how to say it."

I look at the fiddle on the table, which has not been played in at least twelve days. I think about the hard drive in my second bedroom. About the specific cowardice of keeping something close without using it.

"I think," I say, "you say exactly what you said to me. That you were worried about him and you chose the wrong words, and you're sorry."

He looks at me for a long moment. "It sounds simple when you say it."

"It is simple," I say. "It's also incredibly hard. Both things are true."

I don't know what Séamus says to Callum. I know that on Sunday afternoon Eilidh's notebook gets its first call, and by Monday evening we have four musicians who can make it and one who might. I know that on Tuesday Séamus and Callum both appear, separately, at the village hall during what is now being treated as a rehearsal — not together, not warmed up, standing at opposite ends of the space with the caution of two people who have said the necessary things and are not yet sure what shape to give the space between them.

And I know that I am standing at the back of the village hall with a borrowed audio interface, a laptop that I have not opened in over a year, and the headphones with the new ear pads, and my hands are doing the thing where they already know what they're doing before my brain has caught up.

Which is either muscle memory or the other thing. I'm choosing not to examine it.

Priya Gupta drives up from Rothesay with a guitar in a hard case and asks me within thirty seconds about monitoring levels, which makes me like her immediately. Donald Gillies has a bouzouki that he's tuned in the car park. Morag Sinclair sits down at the hall's ancient upright piano and plays a scale and then plays another scale a tone lower and says, "This piano is a disaster," in the cheerful tone of someone who has worked with worse. Eilidh's cousin Rab is twenty-two, nervous, fine on the fiddle — not transcendent, but solid — and keeps glancing at Séamus in a way that's somewhere between starstruck and terrified.

I stand at the back and listen. I take notes. I am noting not sentiment but logistics: the bouzouki is muddy below two hundred hertz, Morag's piano has two dead keys they'll need to know about, Séamus and Callum haven't looked at each other yet but their timing, when they both came in on a chord in the second run-through, locked immediately, like something the decades had trained into their hands separately from whatever was happening in their heads.

After the run-through Callum comes to stand near me. Not talking, just near. He has grey hair and a face like a crumpled map and plays accordion with a gentleness that is slightly at odds with all of that.

"You're not playing," he observes.

"No. I run sound."

"Can't do both?"

"I really can't," I say. "I do one thing."

He considers this. "Fair enough," he says, and goes back.

The third Saturday of June arrives with the particular audacity of Scottish June: completely unexpected warmth, a sky the colour of something expensive, the smell of the sea coming in off the water in a way that is not quite fresh and not quite salt but something between the two that has no good English name and probably has a Gaelic one. The village hall has bunting. Mrs. Paterson is wearing a different Highland cattle jumper, possibly a special occasion version.

I have been here since two in the afternoon, which means I have run cable and argued about monitor placement and fixed one completely incomprehensible feedback loop that turned out to be a ground issue with Donald's DI box. I have drunk three cups of tea that Eilidh appeared with at intervals, each time without comment, which is her way of telling me she's watching.

The band does a sound check at six. It's rough. Morag's two dead piano keys mean a brief consultation about which reels to drop and which to play around. Rab is tense and plays slightly flat when he's nervous, which I note and then quietly ask Priya to compensate for. She nods once. She's already done the math.

Séamus and Callum stand next to each other during the sound check. Not warmly. Not coldly. Like two men who have said the thing that needed saying and are now, carefully, feeling the new shape of things. Their timing still locks. When they find it — the second tune of the set, a reel I don't know the name of but which has a drive to it that makes the hair on my arms do something — I hear the hall fill slightly before the hall actually fills, as if the music is putting out a call.

By eight o'clock the hall is warm and loud and smells of people and whisky and summer, and the band launches into the first set, and someone at the front immediately starts the wrong way in a dashing white sergeant and has to be rescued by three people who know better, and there is laughter, and then the music catches and the laughter becomes movement.

I'm at the sound desk at the back. It is the best view in the house.

There's a moment in the third set — the band is playing Gay Gordons, which I now know the name of having been told it four times today — when Rab's fiddle mic picks up a rattle from a music stand and starts feeding back in the low-mids. No one else would catch it in time. I catch it, cut the frequency, and it's gone in two seconds. Rab doesn't even know it happened. Séamus, from the stage, looks toward the back of the hall. His eyes find me. He does one brief nod.

That is the whole interaction.

At eleven the hall starts to thin, the dancers tired and pleased. The band does a slow air to close — I don't know what it is, only that it feels like something being carefully set down — and then they're done, and there is applause for longer than you'd expect, not out of obligation but because people have saved it up.

I'm coiling cables when Eilidh appears at my elbow. She's been dancing all night; she has the look of someone who has consumed a significant amount of orange squash and physical joy.

"Did it work?" she says.

"You were here."

"I know. But did it work? From your end?"

I think about this properly. The ground loop, fixed. Rab's flat fifths, compensated. Callum's accordion in the monitors just right after the third adjustment. The moment the room temperature and the stage mics conspired to create a proximity issue I saw coming and rerouted before anyone felt it.

"Yeah," I say. "It worked."

She nods, satisfied, and drifts away to help stack chairs.

I go back to the cables. Outside, through the propped-open hall door, the sky above the water is not fully dark — it never fully gets dark here in June, which still gets me every time, this northern light that lingers past any reasonable hour — and somewhere out there the seals are on the south rocks, and the chip van is long closed, and thirty or forty people are leaving a summer ceilidh thinking about next year.

My laptop is open on the sound desk. The session file I've been running tonight is right there on the screen, and underneath it, in the dock, the mixing software I haven't opened in fourteen months.

I close the laptop. I put it in the bag. I pick up the cables.

Then I stop.

I open the laptop again. I open the software. Not the Kingarth session — the other one. The unfinished album. It comes up in the state I left it: half-mixed, the waveforms sitting there in a kind of reproach that turns out, right now, in this particular light, to feel slightly less like a reproach and slightly more like just a thing waiting to be finished.

I look at it for forty-five seconds.

I close it again, properly this time.

But I know what bar I stopped at. And tomorrow I think I'll know what to do with it.

Angus is at the door with his jacket on, and he catches my eye, and he says, "Grand night," and I say, "Grand night," back, meaning it, and we go out into the still-bright Scottish evening together.
