The Tuesday Special

For months, they've shared the same café, the same days, the same careful not-looking — until the menu changes.

Published 7:12 AM SGT·~4 min read

❀

She knew his order by heart.

Mozzarella on sourdough, roasted peppers, no olives — and a small black coffee that he always let go cold before he remembered it was there. Every Tuesday. Every Friday. Same corner table, same unhurried way of settling into the chair like he'd been invited.

Siona had worked this out by accident. She hadn't meant to catalogue him. She'd just noticed, the way you notice a particular song keeps following you into different rooms.

Fratelli's was not a large deli. It was three round tables, a long glass counter full of cured meats and small miracles, and a chalk menu board that Gino changed by hand every morning with the focused expression of a man writing a will. It smelled permanently of rosemary and something buttery that Siona had never identified but that she associated, now, with Tuesday and Friday and that particular corner.

She'd given him a name in her head. She was not proud of this. His name was Probably Taken.

He had the look of someone who would be. Not in a showy way — no expensive watch, no performative gym shoulders. He was just quietly, unfairly put together, with the kind of face that had gotten better at something without announcing it. He read actual books. He argued gently with Gino about football. He tipped well. She knew because the counter was right there and she was not, technically, watching.

This Tuesday, she arrived to find him already at his corner table, which was unusual. He was normally five minutes behind her. She ordered her usual — ricotta toast with honey, sparkling water — and tried not to notice that he was frowning at the menu board.

He was definitely frowning at the menu board.

"Gino," he said, in the tone of a man confronting a minor injustice. "What happened to the roasted peppers?"

Gino, polishing the counter with the serenity of a monk, did not look up. "Supplier."

"The supplier what?"

"Just supplier." Gino gestured at the board as though it explained everything. "Today we have the artichoke."

A pause.

"I don't know how I feel about artichoke."

"You feel good about it," Gino said. "Everyone feels good about it."

Siona, waiting for her sparkling water, looked down at the counter and pressed her lips together. She heard him sigh. She heard the chair push back slightly, as though he were consulting the menu board from a new angle. She heard him say, with the gravity of someone making a significant life decision: "Fine. Artichoke."

"Good man," said Gino, and disappeared into the back.

She should have gone to her table. She had a work thing on her phone. She had a whole lunch hour to get through and a report due Thursday and several completely functional reasons to simply take her water and her ricotta toast and sit down.

She turned slightly toward him instead. Just enough.

"The artichoke is actually good," she said.

He looked up.

It was the first time, she realized, that she had looked at him directly and not immediately looked away. He had grey in his hair that she hadn't clocked from a diagonal. His eyes were doing something she couldn't categorize — some expression between surprised and not-quite-surprised, as if he'd been waiting for someone to say something and had not expected it to be her.

"You've had it?" he said.

"Gino rotates. It comes back every few months. It has capers in it."

"I'm not sure capers are a selling point."

"They're small," she offered. "Easily avoided."

He looked at her for a moment. There was something careful in it — not wary, just deliberate, like a person who'd learned to notice things.

"I've seen you in here," he said. Not accusatory. Just naming something true.

"Tuesday and Friday," she said, because there was no point pretending otherwise.

"Hm." He seemed to find this funny, or close to funny. "Same."

"I know," she said, and then immediately wondered if that was too much. But he didn't seem to find it too much. He was almost smiling — one of those smiles that stays near the corner of the mouth, that you have to catch or it's gone.

Gino materialized from the back and set her water on the counter. He looked between the two of them with the complete blankness of a man who had seen everything and would say nothing.

"I'm Marco," the man said.

"Siona."

"That's not a name I know."

"No one ever does."

"How do you spell it?"

She spelled it for him. He repeated it once, quietly, as though filing it somewhere. There was something strangely careful in that — in the way he said it like it mattered to get it right.

Her toast arrived. His sandwich materialized. They were, she realized, still standing at the counter, which was not the normal configuration of things at Fratelli's. The normal configuration was two separate tables, two separate lunches, the occasional overheard football argument as the only common ground.

She took her plate.

"The capers," he said, "are not as bad as I expected."

She was halfway to her table. She stopped.

"Told you."

"You did." He picked up his coffee. Set it down. Did not drink it. "Same time Friday?"

It was such an ordinary thing to say. It was not ordinary at all.

"I'll be here," Siona said.

And she went to her table, and she opened her phone, and she looked at exactly none of her emails — because outside on the street a bus was pulling away and releasing a small crowd of people, all of them walking in different directions, each one of them going somewhere, the city doing what cities do, and she thought: all of it. All of it is already happening.
