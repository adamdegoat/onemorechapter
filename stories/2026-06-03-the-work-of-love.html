The Work of Love

A wedding coordinator and the groom's chaotic cousin circle each other all day — each too good at not saying the thing they mean.

Published 7:21 AM SGT·~13 min read

❀

Here is something nobody tells you about coordinating weddings: you become the world's best expert on other people's love stories, and you have absolutely no idea what to do with your own.

Kemi Adeyemi has known this about herself for approximately six years. She does not dwell on it. There is always too much to do.

Today, for instance: the florist has delivered blush peonies when the order clearly stated ivory, the string quartet is setting up on the wrong side of the room, and someone — some person — has moved the seating chart from its position on the registration table to directly inside the entrance vestibule where it will be invisible to every arriving guest. All of this before 11am. The wedding is at 2.

Kemi strides across the main hall of The Ironworks with her radio clipped to her blazer and her clipboard held at an angle that communicates, to those who know her, that she is in a state of extreme competence. The venue is beautiful in the way that old industrial spaces become beautiful when someone thoughtful intervenes: exposed brick warm as terracotta, iron pillars wound with fairy lights not yet switched on, windows so high and wide that the July light pours in like something being poured from a jug. It smells of old stone and fresh flowers and the particular metallic sweetness of places that have held a hundred parties and remember all of them.

"Seating chart," she says to her assistant Priya, who materialises at her elbow. "Vestibule."

Priya says something into her own radio and goes.

Kemi turns toward the florals and nearly walks directly into a man carrying an enormous box of what appears to be — she looks twice — prosecco.

"Sorry — absolutely my fault —" He catches the box before it tips. Irish accent, American edges, the look of someone who has been cheerful his whole life and is currently deploying that cheerfulness in a moment of mild crisis. "I was trying to help. The delivery guy was struggling. I just—" He gestures at the box in a way that acknowledges its existence and his ownership of the problem simultaneously. "I don't actually know where this goes."

Kemi takes this in. Tall, dark-haired, wearing a shirt that was probably ironed this morning and has since had a disagreement with the July heat. She does not know him. He is holding forty bottles of prosecco in the middle of her venue and he does not know where they go.

"Who are you?" she asks.

"Declan. Declan Faherty. I'm Ciarán's cousin, from — I'm visiting from New York for the wedding." He shifts the box. "I should have just let the guy carry it. I thought I'd — I was being helpful." He says helpful in a tone that suggests he is currently revising his position on helpfulness as a concept.

Kemi points. "Catering area. Past the third pillar, left."

"Perfect. Great." He doesn't move immediately. "Is the seating chart supposed to be in the vestibule? Only I moved it in there earlier because I thought—"

"It's been sorted," Kemi says, and walks away.

By one o'clock the peonies have been exchanged (ivory, as ordered), the quartet is correctly positioned by the east windows where the acoustics are best, and Kemi has redirected Declan Faherty three more times. Once from the catering prep area, where he had wandered in to ask if anyone needed anything and had nearly knocked over a tiered cake. Once from the back stairs, where he was trying to carry chairs up to the mezzanine for no reason anyone could identify. Once from the bridal suite, where — and this one she would later admit was not entirely his fault — he had knocked on the wrong door looking for his cousin and briefly encountered the bride in her dressing gown.

The bride, Orla, thought it was funny.

"He's always been like this," says Ciarán, who is Kemi's oldest friend and today her client in the technical sense, though she would not use that word with him. He is standing in the corridor outside the groom's room looking at Kemi with the expression he has worn since they were twenty and she started fixing everything he broke. "Lovely bloke. Just — a lot."

"I've noticed," Kemi says. She is adjusting his pocket square. "Are you nervous?"

"Terrified," he says, immediately. "Is that bad?"

"It means you understand what you're doing." She smooths the lapel. "Ciarán. You've been in love with Orla for eight years."

"Seven and a half."

"Seven and a half. You know exactly who you're marrying."

He looks at her, and there is something in his expression she has learned to recognise in grooms in this hour before the ceremony — gratitude, a kind of stilled wonder, the moment when the abstract becomes particular and real. She knows what it means. She has helped thirty-odd couples to this moment. She is extraordinarily good at it.

"You look happy," he tells her.

"I'm always happy at weddings."

"That's not—" He stops. He knows her well enough to know when she is giving the deflection instead of the answer. He does not push. She loves him for that, among other things.

The ceremony is perfect.

Kemi stands at the back of the main hall and watches Orla walk toward Ciarán and sees the precise moment — the one she always watches for — when he stops being nervous. It happens when Orla reaches him and takes his hand without any ceremony, before the celebrant has even begun, as if she cannot wait the additional thirty seconds. He laughs, surprised. She does not. She already knew it would happen exactly like that.

Kemi writes a single note on her clipboard: Remember this one.

She is so busy cataloguing — the light, the angle of the afternoon sun through those high windows, the way the string quartet shifts from Bach into something she can't immediately identify but which is unmistakably right — that she does not notice Declan Faherty arrive beside her until he speaks.

"Is it always like this?" he asks, under his breath. He is watching the ceremony with an expression that is unguarded in the way that very earnest people sometimes are when they don't know they're being observed.

"Like what," she says, very quietly.

He thinks about it. "Like — worth it."

She looks at him sideways. He is not talking about her job.

"Yes," she says. "Always."

He nods once, as if she has confirmed something he needed confirmed, and doesn't say anything else. Which is, Kemi thinks, the most unexpectedly companionable thing he could have done.

At the cocktail hour, he finds her again.

She is not hiding, exactly. She is standing behind the catering station reviewing the dinner timeline and eating a canapé because she has not eaten since seven am and the salmon blinis are extraordinarily good. This is the part of the day she most reliably steals for herself — fifteen minutes while the guests graze and photograph and say I can't believe you're actually married — before the dinner machinery begins.

"Those are the ones with the salmon?" Declan materialises beside her, takes one, eats it, and makes a sound of genuine appreciation. "Unreal. Sorry — am I in your way?"

"You have been in my way consistently since nine-thirty this morning," Kemi says, pleasantly. "But the day is going well regardless."

"Despite my efforts."

"That's not unfair."

He looks at her, and the thing she notices — because she notices things, it is what makes her good at her job and occasionally exhausting to be — is that he is laughing at himself without embarrassment. Most people who make a mess of things spend the rest of the day being overly careful, overcompensating. He is not doing that. He seems entirely willing to simply be someone who moved the seating chart and nearly destroyed a cake and stumbled into the bride's corridor.

"You're incredibly good at this," he says.

"I know," she says.

He blinks. Then laughs. "I meant it as a compliment."

"I took it as one. I just — yes. I am." She sets down the clipboard for approximately thirty seconds, which is unusual for her. "Twelve years of practice."

"You've known Ciarán that long?"

"We met at university. He spilled coffee on my dissertation notes."

"That sounds like him."

"I've been rebuilding from disasters ever since." But she says it warmly, the way you speak about someone who is the best of your mistakes.

Declan leans against the catering table and she realises — slightly too late, slightly too comfortably — that she has stopped wanting him to leave. This is a different thing from what she was feeling at nine-thirty. She takes careful note of it.

"Do you always work at weddings where you know the couple?" he asks.

"Not always. I shouldn't, technically. It's a professional boundary. But Ciarán asked and I—" She stops.

"You couldn't say no."

"I didn't want to say no." Different thing. She is precise about language. "He's been waiting eight years to have this day. I wanted to be the one to make sure it was right."

Declan says nothing for a moment. "Do you ever — I mean, is it strange? Watching people—" He stops. "Sorry, tell me if this is rude."

"Finish it."

"Do you ever watch them and want—" He stops again. She could almost watch him talk himself out of the sentence. "Never mind."

She turns to look at him. "You were going to ask if it's strange to watch them and want it for yourself."

His expression changes. Just slightly. "That was rude."

"It was honest." She picks up the clipboard again. "And yes. Sometimes. Not at every wedding. But sometimes." She does not usually say this. She has a carefully maintained position on the subject, which is that she is too busy and too good at her job and too — something — to have sorted this out, and that is simply the current circumstance and not a problem. But Declan Faherty has wandered into her day and knocked things over and asked an honest question and she finds, unexpectedly, that she has given him an honest answer.

He looks at her for a moment too long.

"Right," she says, and picks up the clipboard with the decisive energy of someone returning to work. "Dinner starts in forty minutes."

The dinner is flawless. The speeches are too long in exactly the right way — Ciarán's best man describes a story involving a tent and a ferry and what he calls a deeply misguided attitude toward weather forecasting that has the whole room howling. Orla's sister cries. Orla does not cry but grips Ciarán's hand under the table in a way Kemi catches from her position near the rear entrance, and adds it to the list of things she is storing.

Declan gives a toast. It is short, which surprises her. He says: "I've never seen Ciarán be still before. When Orla's in the room, he goes still. That seemed important." Sits down. Ciarán says something to him, too quiet for Kemi to catch. Whatever it is, Declan looks at the table and then quickly away, and she thinks: there is more to him than the dropped boxes and the wrong doors.

There is always more, of course. This is something coordinating weddings teaches you irrevocably: there is always more to every person than the version of them you first meet. She knows this in a professional, practiced way. She knows it differently, tonight, watching him.

She does not examine this distinction carefully.

After dinner, when the floor is cleared and the dancing begins and the fairy lights finally come on — casting The Ironworks in a gold she has specifically designed across three planning meetings — Priya finds her near the bar.

"You should take a break," Priya says. "Everything's running."

"I'm fine."

"Kemi."

She looks at the dance floor. Orla has pulled Ciarán into something that is not the choreographed first dance but something messier and more honest, and he is laughing at her footwork, and she is not stopping. Around them the other guests are beginning to fill the space.

Declan is dancing with someone's grandmother. He is making a complete mess of it and the grandmother is delighted.

Kemi says, to Priya: "He flies back to New York tomorrow."

Priya follows her gaze. "Oh. Early?"

"I heard him tell Ciarán. Seven am." She keeps her voice even. "Just observing."

Priya, who has worked with Kemi for three years and knows better than to say most things, says nothing.

At eleven-thirty, the guests begin to drift. By midnight the main hall is emptying, couples slow-dancing or finding their coats, children asleep in corners, the particular soft entropy of a wedding reaching its natural end. Kemi sends Priya home. The venue team can take the structural breakdown; her job tonight is linens, florals, the small personal items left on tables — the things that need a careful eye.

She is alone at a round table near the east window, sorting through abandoned favour boxes and a pair of sunglasses and one truly inexplicable single glove, when she hears footsteps.

Declan. He is still in his jacket. He looks like someone who made a decision.

"You're still here," she says.

"My cab's coming at six." He stops a few feet away. "I wanted to—" He moves a hand through his hair. "I keep almost saying things to you today and then not saying them."

"I noticed."

"I know. That's—" He exhales. "Here's the thing. I'm bad at this. I talk too much when I'm nervous and I moved the seating chart and I nearly demolished a three-hundred-pound cake, and I don't actually know you at all — you've basically just been this incredibly competent person appearing to tell me where to put things all day — but I keep thinking about what you said at the cocktail hour."

Kemi sets down the sunglasses. "Which part."

"The sometimes." He looks at her directly. It costs him something, she can see that, the looking without the deflection. "You said sometimes you watch the couples and want it for yourself. I wanted to say — I do too. Not just watching. I've been — I moved to New York because I thought it would sort itself out, the wanting, if I just kept going. And it's been three years and I've been going and it hasn't sorted itself out at all." He stops. "I'm not telling you this because I think—" Another stop. "I know I'm on a flight at seven. I know this is insane. I just didn't want to leave without saying that today was the first time in a while I felt like I was in the right room." He pauses. "That probably sounds—"

"No," Kemi says.

He is quiet.

She looks at him for a long moment. The fairy lights are still on — someone forgot to switch them, or she forgot to tell them to, one of those two — and the room smells of flowers and wine and the candle wax of a long evening. Ciarán and Orla are long gone to their hotel. The Ironworks is holding its breath.

"I have Ciarán's number," she says. "So do you."

"Yes."

"That's not nothing."

He looks at her and the corner of his mouth moves — uncertain and real, nothing performed in it. "That's not nothing," he agrees.

She picks up her clipboard. It is automatic. Then she sets it back down.

"There are forty people's love stories I can tell you about from memory," she says. "I know which ones I got right and which ones I almost got wrong and I know exactly what makes the difference. I know every sign." She looks at him. "I am currently refusing to examine what that means."

"Okay," he says. He says it the way you say a word that is standing in for something much larger.

She walks to the east window and he follows her. Below, through the glass, South London hums in the dark — a cab turning a corner, someone's music bleeding from a late-lit flat, the July night warm and close and going nowhere in a hurry.

"Tell me something true," she says. "Not about the flight. Not about New York. Something you actually think."

He is quiet for a moment, and she can almost see him sorting through the options, the deflections, the funny answers.

"I think," he says, finally, "that the worst thing I could do right now is let tonight be a story I tell at someone else's wedding."

Kemi is still looking at the window. She says, very quietly: "Then don't."
