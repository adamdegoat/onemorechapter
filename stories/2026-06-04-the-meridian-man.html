The Meridian Man

Two art world rivals finally meet in person at a London gallery opening — and discover the argument they've been having across print was only the beginning.

Published 7:12 AM SGT·~11 min read

❀

"You acquired the Salcedo wrong."

That was the first thing Reuben Makinde said to me, in person, after four years of acquiring things at each other. Not hello. Not you must be Yael. Just straight to the wound.

I turned from the painting I'd been studying — a small, brutal oil by an Edinburgh artist we'd nearly lost to him the previous autumn — and found him standing a half-step closer than strangers usually stand. He had a glass of white wine in each hand, which meant he'd brought one for me, which meant he'd already spotted me, which meant he'd had to decide to come over.

"I acquired it correctly," I said. "You acquired it badly."

"You paid thirty percent over estimate." He offered me the wine. I took it.

"I paid what it was worth. You paid what the estimate said it was worth, which is a different and smaller thing." I drank. The wine was too cold. "The estimate was written by your dealer, incidentally."

"My dealer is one of the most respected —"

"Your dealer is representing you in the room where valuations happen. I'm not saying he's dishonest. I'm saying the structure creates a ceiling you keep bumping your head on." I looked at him properly then, the way you look at something you've heard described but never seen: the wire-rimmed glasses he wore in every photograph, the close-cropped hair going silver at the temples rather earlier than thirty-six should produce, the dark suit that fit as though it had been made for an occasion and this happened to be it. "It's nice to meet you, by the way."

He blinked. Then laughed — a real one, short and surprised. "Reuben Makinde."

"I know."

"Yael Chen-Goldberg."

"I know that too," I said. "I've been reading your reviews since the Lim retrospective."

"You disagreed with my review of the Lim retrospective."

"Publicly and at length, yes. That's different from not reading it."

The Forde Gallery is six rooms in a converted Clerkenwell printworks, and on opening nights it fills with exactly the sort of people who stand very close to the art and say little about it directly. I liked it for that reason. The crowd was small enough to move through, large enough to get lost in, and the Edinburgh artist whose work hung on these particular walls had a quality I was still learning to name — something about refusal. The paintings refused to be beautiful easily. They made you earn it.

Reuben and I drifted through two rooms without deciding to drift together. It happened the way currents happen: not pulled, exactly, more that we were moving in the same direction and it was less effort than parting.

"The large triptych," he said, in front of it. "You're going to bid on it."

"Probably."

"We're going to bid on it."

"Then we'll find out what it's worth." I tilted my head. The centre panel had a quality of held breath, a pale interior with a figure turning away, caught in the mid-motion of leaving. "Where would you put it?"

"New main gallery, first floor. Natural light from the north." He described it precisely — the dimensions, the wall, the sightline from the entrance — and I had the uneasy experience of imagining it there and finding the imagining good.

"That's actually right," I said.

"Most things I say are actually right. You just don't have to admit it in print."

"I admit it when it's true. The Barlow review was right. The position on Meridian's emerging programme is right. The Salcedo acquisition was wrong."

"You're going to keep coming back to the Salcedo."

"I'm going to keep coming back to the Salcedo."

He turned to look at me rather than the painting, which in a gallery is a kind of declaration. "Why? It was a single acquisition. You won. What does it matter how I lost?"

I thought about that. Outside, through the high industrial windows, London was doing its orange nighttime thing, the sky not dark but amber and strange. "Because the how matters," I said finally. "If you'd lost because we simply wanted it more, that would be one story. If you lost because the structure around you has a flaw — that's a different story, and it has sequels."

He was quiet for a moment. A couple passed behind us, their voices low, discussing something in the painting with the over-careful enthusiasm of people who want to be seen discussing art.

"You've been paying attention," he said.

"I've been paying attention for four years. You're not easy to ignore."

The words landed somewhere between us and stayed there.

We ended up at the far end of the last room, where a bench sat against the wall beneath a single small canvas — a window, painted from inside, looking out onto something unspecified and grey. The bench existed at an angle to everything else, set back, slightly peripheral. The sort of spot where you go when you want the room but not the middle of it.

He sat first. I sat at the appropriate distance — the museum-bench distance, the professional distance, close enough for conversation and not close enough for anything else.

"Tell me about the Whitfield programme," he said. "Not the press release version."

"Why would I tell you the real version?"

"Because you're curious what I'd say about it, and the press release version won't make me say anything interesting."

That was irritatingly accurate. I turned the wine glass in my hands. The wine had warmed to the right temperature. "We're trying to do something harder than we've done before," I said. "Not just collect boldly — anyone with money can collect boldly. We want to change what the conversation is. What questions the work is asking. We have three pieces in negotiation right now that, if they go through, will mean something." I paused. "I'm not going to tell you which three."

"I know which three."

"Then you don't need me to tell you."

"No," he said. "But I was interested in how you'd describe them. The framing."

"The framing is that there's a generation of artists working in the gap between what documentation can do and what presence can do, and that gap is the most interesting aesthetic territory in twenty years, and nobody is acquiring systematically into it yet." I looked at him. "Now you."

He was quiet for a moment, in the way of someone deciding something. "The Meridian programme is —" He stopped. Started again. "Was. The Meridian programme was a version of what you're describing but coming from a different angle. Less documentation, more materiality. The same territory, entered from the north."

I noticed the tense. "Was."

He looked at the small canvas above us. The painted window. "I'm leaving Meridian."

"I know there's been speculation —"

"Not speculation. I've signed. I'm taking the programme director role at the Aldrin Foundation in New York. I move in five weeks."

The room continued around us. Someone laughed across the gallery, a warm and carrying sound. I found that I was very still.

"Five weeks," I said.

"I've been planning it for about a year."

A year. All of this last year — the competing bids, the duelling reviews, the long argument we'd been having across print about the future of collecting practice — he'd been planning to leave. Not pausing, not circling, not wondering whether to stay. Already gone, essentially. Already in New York, already redirected. The Reuben Makinde I had been thinking about, reading, disagreeing with, half-wanting to meet in a room exactly like this one — that Reuben Makinde was a version that would exist for five more weeks and then stop.

"Tonight is a goodbye party," I said.

"For London, yes." A beat. "I didn't know you'd be here."

"Would it have changed anything?"

He looked at me then, directly, and I noticed that the wire-rimmed glasses were slightly scratched on the left lens — not new, an old scratch, the kind you stop seeing on your own things. "No," he said. "But I would have come sooner."

We stayed on the bench for a long time. Long enough that the room thinned around us, the crowd migrating toward the front where someone was attempting a small speech. We didn't migrate. We talked about the materiality argument versus the documentation argument in the way that two people talk when they've been waiting to have the conversation and have only just found themselves in the same room to have it. He was wrong about two things and right about one large thing, and I told him both. He returned the favour.

At some point our professional distance shrank to something else. Not dramatically — not in the way that requires acknowledgment — but I was aware of it. The way I was aware of the cedar and cold night air coming through a gap in the high windows. The way I was aware of his hands when he gestured, which he did when the argument moved him, and it moved him often.

"The Whitfield will miss you," he said at one point. "When you eventually leave."

"I'm not leaving."

"Not yet." He said it without cruelty, as observation. "But you'll get an offer like mine in three years, maybe four. Something you can't build in London. And you'll go."

"Or I'll build it in London."

"Maybe." He didn't sound unconvinced, exactly — more like he was holding space for both futures, which I found, for reasons I didn't examine, unexpectedly moving. "You're the kind of person who makes the place rather than waiting for the place to be made. That's rarer than you know."

"You're trying to flatter me into letting you have the triptych."

"I'm trying to tell you something true. The triptych is separate."

The triptych was separate. I looked at him, at the scratched lens, at the slight loosening of the suit jacket where he'd pushed his sleeves up an hour ago, and I thought about the year he'd spent planning to leave while I'd spent it planning to make him lose. There was something almost funny about it. There was something almost not funny about it at all.

"What does New York think it's getting?" I asked.

"Someone who knows how to build a collection with an argument behind it."

"They're right about that."

"High praise from Yael Chen-Goldberg."

"I give it where it's owed."

He turned, not much — a degree of turning, the kind that narrows the remaining distance. His elbow was on the back of the bench. Mine wasn't quite touching it. The speech from the front room had ended. The laughter was general now, the particular looseness of a gathering winding toward its last hour.

"If I weren't leaving," he said, "if I had, say, the three years you have before you get your own offer — I think we'd have ended up at a lot of these things." He gestured, minimally, at the room. "Arguing."

"Arguing," I agreed.

"And occasionally being right in the same direction."

"That would have been —" I chose the word carefully, because he'd been choosing words carefully all evening and it seemed like a courtesy to return. "— significant."

"Yes." He didn't add to it. He left it there, significant, which was exactly the right decision.

Near eleven, the gallery lights warmed to their closing signal. We stood — the returning to full height of people who have been sitting at the museum-bench distance for two hours and haven't acknowledged what that distance became.

By the door, someone had retrieved his coat. Black wool, long, the kind of coat that London produces and then doesn't need anymore when you go somewhere that has a different relationship with cold. He put it on. I watched him put it on. I thought about the Salcedo and the triptych and the year he'd spent already gone and whether it would have mattered, the timeline, if either of us had moved differently inside it.

It wouldn't have. That's the circumstantial cruelty of it. There was no miscommunication to correct, no wall to melt, no moment where a different choice would have redirected things. He was already New York's. He'd been New York's before I walked into this room. The only thing that had changed tonight was that we'd found each other in the same room and discovered, too precisely and too late, the particular shape of what we might have argued about for years.

I buttoned my coat. He watched me do it.

"The triptych," he said.

"I'm going to acquire it."

"I know." He smiled, which was different from laughing — quieter, more settled. "I'm going to try to acquire it first, out of professional obligation, and then when I don't, I'm going to write about where you put it."

"I'll read it."

"I know you will." He held the door. "You always read what I write."

"For the same reason I read anything I disagree with," I said. "Because not reading it would mean I'd stopped thinking."

He looked at me for one more moment in the cold Clerkenwell air, London spread behind him in its amber and amber, and I understood that we were at the end of the conversation and that the end of the conversation was also the end of something that had barely started and was, in a year's time, likely to ache.

"Tell me something honestly," he said. "Before I go."

"I've been honest all evening."

"One more thing."

I considered him. The scratched lens. The cold air. The particular quality of the evening, which had refused to be beautiful easily and made me earn it. "All right," I said.

He nodded, once, as though I'd agreed to something larger. And then: "The next person who writes a duelling review of your programme — the person who spends four years getting your Salcedos wrong —" He paused. "Be in the same room with them sooner."

I breathed in the cold air. I let it settle.

"That's not a question," I said.

"No," said Reuben Makinde, turning toward the street. "But it's still honest."
