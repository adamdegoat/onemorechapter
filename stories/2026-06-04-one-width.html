One Width

Nine-year-old Dora notices a boy sitting very still at the edge of the pool — and decides, practically, to do something about it.

Published 7:12 AM SGT·~5 min read

❀

Later, when people asked Ana what she had seen from the pool steps, she said she had watched her granddaughter boss a teenager into the best moment of his summer. She was not wrong.

The pool opened at eight, and Dora was always there by five past.

This was a point of pride. The Piscina Municipal do Restelo sat between a stand of umbrella pines and a low white wall, and in July the water was cool for exactly the first hour before the sun found it properly. Dora knew this. She was nine, and she knew exactly three things with absolute certainty: the pool was best before nine, her grandmother made the second-best pastéis de nata in all of Lisbon (the best were from a place on Rua Augusta that Ana refused to acknowledge), and swimming was not difficult if you simply did it.

She was halfway through her sixth length when she saw the boy.

He was sitting on the steps at the shallow end. Not in the pool — on the steps, in his shorts, with his feet barely submerged. He had the look of someone trying very hard to appear comfortable. He was not comfortable. His uncle was already doing laps in the next lane, a loose, easy crawl, entirely unaware that his nephew had not moved in ten minutes.

Dora stopped at the wall. She looked at the boy. The boy looked at the water.

She climbed out.

"You don't know how," she said.

The boy looked up. He was maybe fourteen, with sunburned shoulders and the expression of someone who had just been told something embarrassing in front of the whole school.

"I know how," he said.

"You've been sitting there since I started my sixth length."

"I'm resting."

"You weren't in the water."

He opened his mouth, closed it. His name, she would learn, was Marcos. He lived in Amadora and had never had a pool nearby, and the one time he'd tried at a cousin's place in Setúbal two summers ago there had been an incident he preferred not to think about. He was fourteen. Being afraid of water when you were fourteen was — he had already worked this out — approximately a thousand times more embarrassing than being afraid of water when you were four.

"I'm Dora," she said, sitting down next to him on the steps as though she had been invited. "The shallow end is one metre. I can stand up here. You probably can too."

He was, in fact, considerably taller than her. He looked down at the water. One metre of perfectly blue water, lit by a sun that was already doing serious work on the tiles around the pool's edge.

"I'm fine," he said.

"You look like you're about to do a maths exam."

He almost laughed. Almost.

Dora was not, her grandmother would later tell the story, a particularly empathetic child in the conventional sense. She did not pick up on atmosphere. She did not read the room. What she did was identify a problem and assume it was solvable, with roughly the same energy she applied to a stuck jar lid.

"Put your feet flat on the bottom," she said. "Just stand there. That's it."

Marcos stood. The water came to just below his knee. He breathed.

"See?"

"I wasn't worried about that part."

"What part were you worried about?"

He didn't answer. She waited. At nine, Dora had no instinct for the kindness of letting something go.

"The going under part," he said, finally.

"You don't have to go under. You just have to go across." She pointed to the opposite wall. Four metres. The width of the pool. "Walk it first if you want."

"I'm not going to walk it."

"Because that would be embarrassing?"

A pause. "Yes."

"Okay," she said, as though this were completely reasonable, which of course it was. "Then we do it properly."

She showed him three times how to put his face in the water and breathe out through his nose. She demonstrated the arms. She stood next to him in the shallows and said, with the calm authority of someone who had been swimming lengths since she was six, that he should not think about the depth, the water, or anything at all, actually — just move his arms and kick his feet and go.

"That's your whole advice."

"That's the whole advice."

His uncle had stopped at the far wall and was watching now, though he had the sense not to say anything.

Marcos kicked off from the wall.

It was not elegant. It was not smooth. He went slightly diagonal, he swallowed some water at the halfway point, and he made a noise that Dora would describe to her grandmother as "like a car that won't start." But he kept going. The opposite wall arrived faster than he expected. His hand touched tile.

He stood up, gasping, water streaming off him, blinking in the hot Lisbon light.

He had swum one width of the pool. Just one.

Dora was already there — she'd walked around the edge — looking at him with the expression of someone who has just completed a mildly interesting experiment.

"See?" she said.

Marcos wiped his face. He looked back at the wall he'd started from. Four metres. Not nothing.

"That's it?" he said.

"That's it," she said. "Then you do it again."

On the pool steps, Ana lowered her book. She watched her granddaughter turn and walk back to the other side without ceremony, watched the boy stand in the water for a moment, looking at the distance, recalibrating.

Then she heard Marcos call after Dora across the shimmering water.

"Hey. How many lengths do you do?"

Dora didn't turn around. "Twelve. But I've been doing this longer than you."

"How long?"

She glanced back, considering. "Three years."

A pause.

"I'll do two widths," he said.

"Good start," she said, and got back in the water.
