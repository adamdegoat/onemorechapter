What the Dough Knows

Bea moved to Melbourne for a job that vanished. Filling her days at a food bank kitchen, she finds the work she didn't know she needed.

Published 7:12 AM SGT·~14 min read

❀

There were twenty-three industrial trays stacked against the far wall of the Fitzroy Food Bank kitchen, and nobody had counted them in at least three weeks.

Bea Kowalski knew this because she had counted them herself on her first morning, when she arrived at 7:45 and the coordinator hadn't yet unlocked the storage room and she had absolutely nothing else to do with her hands. Twenty-three. She wrote it on a Post-it note and stuck it to the tray rack, which felt both useful and slightly unhinged in equal measure.

That was six weeks ago. Now it was Tuesday, and the Post-it was still there, and the count was wrong — down to nineteen now, she suspected, because someone kept running the last four through the dishwasher without checking whether they were oven-safe — and Bea had learned that this kitchen operated on a system of benevolent chaos that was somehow, inexplicably, not her problem to fix.

She was still working on accepting that.

"Okay, people, today's incoming —" Marcus, the coordinator, read from his phone while simultaneously attempting to pour himself a coffee, which meant the phone was wedged between his ear and shoulder and he kept having to stop and read it again. He was thirty-something, had the harried energy of a man who slept six hours on a good night, and wore the same blue lanyard every day. "We've got an oversupply of preserved lemons from the Lygon Street deli, um, forty-seven kilograms of potatoes from the Vic Market donation, some day-old bread assorted — a lot of it — and someone has dropped off what the note calls 'the muffins from Glenroy,' which I'm told are fine but I'm also told there was an incident."

"What kind of incident," said Renata, not looking up from where she was already washing potatoes with methodical determination. Renata was sixty-one, Croatian, had been volunteering here for nine years, and communicated mostly through skeptical interrogation. She and Bea had, by the fourth week, developed a mutual respect that neither of them had explicitly acknowledged and neither of them planned to.

"The note doesn't elaborate," said Marcus. "So: bread team, could we get through three hundred portions today? We've got the St. Kilda families coming at noon and the Collingwood walk-ins at three."

Bea was already at the bench.

This was what she did: she showed up, she found a task, and she executed it with the focused efficiency of someone who had trained six days a week for four years at a patisserie in Kazimierz and then two more at a restaurant in Warsaw before the job in Melbourne had dissolved — just dissolved, like a dream she couldn't quite reconstruct in daylight — and left her with the lease on a flat in Northcote, a suitcase of professional knives, and a lot of mornings to fill.

She wasn't here to find herself. She was here so she didn't spend the day refreshing job listing sites while eating biscuits over the sink. The food bank was something to do. That was the entire reason.

The bread situation was immediately a problem.

There were six different kinds — sourdough, multigrain, two types of Turkish flatbread, some sort of brioche that had seen better days, and a rye she recognized by smell before she even opened the bag. She stood in front of the delivery and thought: these cannot all be treated the same way, and everyone is currently treating them all the same way.

"We usually just slice everything," said a young man named Dev, who had joined the volunteer roster two weeks before Bea and therefore treated her as a veteran, which she found both flattering and mortifying. Dev was twenty-two, did something with databases, and had the slightly over-eager quality of someone who deeply wanted to help but wasn't always sure of the best mechanism. He was currently holding a bread knife in a way that suggested he had not often held bread knives.

"The Turkish bread doesn't want slicing," Bea said. "It wants tearing. If you slice it, it compresses. The families should be able to tear it themselves."

Dev looked at the bread. Looked at Bea. "Oh," he said. "Yeah. That makes sense." He set down the knife with visible relief.

"And the rye —" she started.

"Is rye popular?" He seemed genuinely uncertain, the way someone is when they want to be helpful but the gap between their enthusiasm and their knowledge is uncomfortably large.

"With some people very much yes," Bea said. "Separate it. Label it. Don't mix it in with the sourdough or people won't know what they're getting."

She said it perhaps more sharply than she meant to. Not rudely, but with the clipped economy of someone who considered unnecessary imprecision a form of waste. Dev nodded and started pulling the rye loaves to one side, murmuring "label, okay, label," under his breath as though memorizing a mantra.

By mid-morning, Marcus had gently suggested that Bea might want to ease up on reorganizing the labeling system entirely, because some of the labels were Lorraine's system, and Lorraine had been running labels since 2019, and Lorraine would be in at noon.

Bea looked at the current labeling system, which used a combination of masking tape, permanent marker, and what appeared to be three different font sizes without discernible logic.

"I will be very respectful of Lorraine's system," she said, in a tone that suggested she would be very respectful of Lorraine's system.

The preserved lemons were a gift. An actual gift, not a problem disguised as one.

Bea stood over the crate and felt something shift slightly in her chest. Forty-seven kilograms of good preserved lemons — she could smell them through the bags, that bright fermented salt-citrus smell — and a kitchen with industrial ovens that had been, she noticed, underused. Most of the cooking here was assembly rather than preparation. Sandwiches, portioned meals from donated tins, reheated dishes from partner restaurants. The ovens sat largely idle before the noon delivery.

She found Marcus between phone calls.

"If I make something," she said, "with the lemons. If I use today's overbread — the brioche especially, it's going to go bad — and the lemons, and whatever else is available. Something people can take home. Individually portioned." She paused. "I'm a pastry chef. I know what I'm doing."

Marcus blinked. He had the look of a man being asked to approve something he hadn't anticipated and couldn't immediately identify a reason to refuse. "How long?"

"Two hours. Maybe less."

He checked his phone. He checked the oven schedule, which lived on a laminated sheet held to the fridge with a magnet shaped like a tram. "The big oven's free till eleven-thirty."

"That's enough," Bea said.

She didn't wait for further confirmation.

What she made was something between a bread pudding and a tart — a flat, pressed thing, not elegant in presentation but exact in construction. She tore the brioche by hand, soaked it in an egg mixture made from the donated eggs they always had too many of, folded in the preserved lemon in strips, added a base of flattened bread for structure, pressed it into portioning trays, and topped it with a rough sugar crust made from the catering granules in the dry goods cupboard. The whole thing went into the oven at 180 and she stood at the counter and did not hover — she was not a hoverer — but she tracked the time the way she always did, by feel and smell and the quality of the heat coming back at her when she briefly opened the door at twenty minutes.

Dev appeared beside her at some point and watched in silence for a moment. "What is that."

"Szarlotka variant," Bea said. "Sort of. Adapted."

"What's a szarlotka."

"Polish apple cake. This isn't that, but the structure is similar." She paused. "Without the apples. And with lemons. So not very similar, actually."

Dev considered this. "It smells incredible," he said, with the openness of someone who genuinely meant it and hadn't learned to understate.

It did smell incredible. That was just fact. The sugar was caramelizing correctly, the lemon had gone soft and aromatic, the egg had set the brioche into something with density and chew. It would not win any plating competitions. It was the kind of thing a competent person made in a rush with what was available, which was a different skill from the kind of thing that won plating competitions, and Bea had both.

Renata came and stood in the kitchen doorway and looked at the trays for a long time without saying anything.

"It's fine," Bea said, not defensively. Just stating.

"I know it's fine," Renata said. "I'm smelling it."

When it came out and she cut it into portions — forty-eight pieces, she cut precisely, because waste was waste — and wrapped each one individually in the paper squares they used for sandwiches, she labeled them in both English and, after a brief internal argument with herself, a small note she added in Polish. Then she stood back and thought: there will be people here this afternoon who might never have eaten anything like this, and there will also be people who will know exactly what this is, and that was fine, that was more than fine, that was the whole point of having forty-seven kilograms of preserved lemons and an idle oven and four years of training that you were currently using to fill your days.

The Collingwood walk-ins at three included a woman named Anya, who came every week, who spoke to Renata in a mixture of Croatian and Ukrainian that neither of them was entirely fluent in but that both of them somehow navigated, and who picked up the wrapped portion and looked at it and said something to Renata.

Renata relayed: "She says it looks like something her grandmother made."

Bea, who was already wiping down the second bench and not looking up, said: "What region."

After a translation exchange: "Lviv."

Bea thought about that for a moment. "Different method," she said. "Same idea, probably."

Anya said something else and Renata translated with what appeared to be mild amusement: "She says the smell is right even if the method is different."

Bea looked up then. Anya was maybe sixty, had the face of someone who had carried a lot and was currently having an unremarkable Tuesday afternoon in a food bank in Collingwood, which was also its own kind of good day. She was holding the wrapped portion against her chest like it was something to protect.

Bea nodded once. Anya nodded once. They left it there.

The message from Kraków came on a Thursday, two weeks later, while Bea was on the tram home from the food bank with flour on her forearm and a bag of slightly battered dragon fruit that Marcus had encouraged everyone to take because they were one day out from being donated.

Chef Marta Głowacka. The Nowa restaurant. A position as senior pastry chef — not second, senior — at a place that Bea had read about in Gastronomia magazine before she was old enough to have a career. Starting September. The email was formal and correct and said, in Marta Głowacka's characteristic understated way, that they had been following her previous work and believed she was ready.

Bea sat on the tram and read it three times. The city went by outside: a mural on a building in Smith Street, a Lebanese bakery with its lights still on at six in the evening, a man walking a very small dog with an air of profound dignity.

Ready. She was ready. She had been ready for several years, in fact; the timing had simply not cooperated.

She got home and put the dragon fruit in the fridge and stood in her Northcote kitchen, which was good-sized by Melbourne standards and which she had made comfortable in the way she made everywhere comfortable — organised the dry goods, bought a decent knife block, taped a list of market schedules to the inside of the pantry door. She was not someone who drifted through spaces. She arranged them.

She called her sister in Kraków. Her sister, who was thirty-four and sensible and worked in logistics, said: "Bea. This is what you wanted."

"I know," Bea said.

"When did you start wanting something else?"

Bea looked at her pantry list. She thought about Renata's skeptical interrogation and the twenty-three trays (currently eighteen) and Dev carefully separating rye from sourdough and murmuring label, label to himself. She thought about Anya holding the paper-wrapped portion against her chest.

"I don't know," she said. "That's the problem."

"Is it a problem," her sister said, not unkindly.

She told Marcus on a Monday, which was the quiet day, when only the core volunteers came in and the kitchen had a different rhythm — slower, less urgent, the radio turned up slightly. She told him she had a job offer in Poland and she was thinking about it.

Marcus put down his phone, which was significant.

"When do you need to decide?"

"Three weeks."

He nodded. He didn't say: we need you, or: you've been so valuable, or any of the things that would have made the decision tidier. He said: "Is it a good job?"

"Yes," Bea said. "It's a very good job."

"Okay," he said. And then: "We'd put a good word in for you. Anywhere. If that's useful."

It was such a Marcus thing to say — practical, undramatic, already thinking about the logistics of her leaving — that Bea almost laughed.

She told Dev when he came in at ten, because Dev had a transparent face and he was clearly going to notice that something was happening, so she may as well be direct. He said, "Oh," and then, after a pause: "Is Melbourne — I mean, has it been — was it okay? Here?"

He didn't know how to ask what he was asking. She could see him trying to arrange the words into a shape that didn't feel intrusive.

"Yes," she said. "It has been okay here."

"Good," he said, with visible relief. "That's — yeah. Good."

She told Renata last, and Renata looked at her for a moment with an expression that was not readable in any simple way, and said: "You'll keep the recipe?"

"The lemon thing?"

"Write it down properly. Don't carry it only in your head. Things get lost."

Bea thought of all the recipes she had in her head — her grandmother's poppy seed roll, a cream cake she had learned at nineteen and adapted twice, a pastry she had developed entirely herself in Warsaw and never written down because she thought she would always be there to make it. "Yes," she said. "I'll write it down."

Three weeks was not enough time to know. That was what Bea kept returning to, in her flat in Northcote, with the dragon fruit in the fridge and the knife block on the counter and the market schedule on the pantry door. Three weeks was not enough to know whether Melbourne was becoming something real or whether it was still the holding pattern it had started as — the thing she did while she waited for her actual life to resume.

She had planned to decide by Tuesday. Tuesday became Wednesday. She made the lemon pastry again in her own kitchen, scaling it down, writing each step as she went, cross-referencing the quantities in grams, the temperature in Celsius, a note about the eggs. Donated eggs work well — slightly variable in size, adjust by feel. She wrote that in and considered it. Then she left it in.

On Thursday she emailed Chef Marta Głowacka. The email took four drafts. The final version was eight sentences and said, with clarity and without excessive explanation, that she was grateful for the offer and deeply honoured by the consideration and was not in a position to accept at this time, but asked if there was any possibility of revisiting in the following year.

She did not know if that was the right answer. She suspected there was no right answer, only the one she had chosen, and the shape it made in her life going forward.

She sent it before she could draft a ninth sentence.

Friday morning at the food bank, there was a delivery of overripe stone fruit from a Dandenong distributor — nectarines and white peaches, none of them suitable for direct distribution, all of them suitable for something else. Bea stood over the crates and the warm, oversweet smell came up at her like an argument, and she thought: tarte tatin logic, or possibly a compote for the bread, or — with the right ratio — something to set overnight in the portioning trays that the families could eat cold on a summer morning.

Melbourne in June was mild and the kitchen windows were open and somewhere on Brunswick Street a tram went past with that specific metal-on-metal sound she had stopped noticing, which meant she had started hearing it as a given, which was its own kind of information about the last three months.

Marcus walked in, phone in hand, lanyard swinging. "What are we doing with the stone fruit."

"Give me an hour," Bea said.

He already had his phone back up. "You've got ninety minutes before the Carlton crew comes in."

Ninety minutes was better than an hour. She reached for a knife.
