The Gate Is Shut

A pre-dawn squall scatters an entire rose garden across a Scots village street — and without anyone asking, the whole street turns up to fix it.

Published 7:22 AM SGT·~5 min read

❀

Haar does something strange to sound. It doesn't muffle it so much as flatten it — takes the edges off, makes the world feel padded and interior, like being inside a jar. I've walked this street before six every morning for twenty years and I know the difference between a haar morning and a clear one before I've opened the bakery door. This morning the lamplight doesn't cast shadows, it just spreads, goes soft and directionless, and the cobbles are dark with moisture even though it hasn't rained.

I'm wheeling the delivery trolley — bread rolls for the post office, a white loaf for Mrs. Gillies at number four, a box of morning rolls for the harbour café — when I see them. At first I think the haar is playing tricks. The pale shapes scattered across the main street look wrong, like litter someone has arranged too deliberately. Then I smell them. Roses. Hundreds of them, loose heads and full stems both, plastered across the pavement in every direction. Old Henderson's rambling rose covers the whole front wall of his place and runs along the garden gate, and last night's squall has torn it apart. The gate is shut. The house dark. Henderson's visiting his daughter in Inverness.

I stop the trolley.

I think: the gate is shut. I think: the roses are going to be mulched to brown by the time the first cars come through. I think: I've got deliveries.

I leave the trolley against the post office wall and start picking them up.

I'm not making a decision. My hands are just doing it. I grab stems and heads together and pile them in the crook of my arm, working from the kerb inward. The roses are cold from the night air, wet, some of them half-open, some flattened where they've been trodden. They smell extraordinary — that particular deep rose smell that gets stronger when they're damaged.

I hear footsteps and glance up. It's Duncan Farquhar, in his wax jacket and his old rubber-soled boots, heading for the quay like he does every morning of his retired life even though he hasn't worked a boat in twelve years. He stops. Looks at the roses. Looks at me.

He doesn't say anything. He just tucks his cap into his pocket, crouches with the deliberateness of a man whose knees are unreliable, and starts picking.

We work without talking. That's fine. Duncan and I have never needed to fill silences.

The school bus doesn't come until seven-fifteen but Callum Begg and Jay Sutherland are always early because Callum's mum pushes him out the house. They come around the corner with their bags hanging off one shoulder each, deep in some argument, and they stop dead. Jay looks at the roses, looks at us, and I see the exact moment he decides. He drops his bag on top of a postbox. Callum groans — I can hear it from where I am — then drops his bag too.

"Don't pick the ones that are totally crushed," I tell them. "They'll just fall apart."

"Right," says Callum.

They work fast, teenagers being competitive about everything, picking up armfuls at a time and making a pile near the postbox. They go back and forth. Jay finds a stem that's still got a bud on it and holds it up like he's made a discovery.

"That's a good one," I say.

"Aye." He puts it carefully on top of the pile.

Rhona Mackay comes out of the close at the top of the street with her spaniel, Midge, pulling at the lead. She sees us. She takes the lead and ties Midge to the lamppost — Midge sits like he's been briefed — and Rhona starts picking up roses with both hands, quickly, not making anything of it, the way you'd clear broken glass.

By half six the pavement is clear. We've worked our way from the far end back to Henderson's gate, and now we're standing in front of it with this enormous heap of roses and nowhere obvious to put them.

Duncan looks at the gate. "He'll not be back till the weekend."

"He won't want them anyway," I say. "Half of them are broken."

"What do we do with them?"

I look at the pile. It's absurd, really — hundreds and hundreds of roses, cold and wet and smelling like a flower market at closing time. The haar has thinned a little while we worked and the light is starting to go grey-pink over the water.

"Take them," I say. "Take whatever you want."

So that's what we do. Callum and Jay take big armfuls each — Jay's going to look remarkable on the school bus — and Rhona unties Midge and takes as many as she can manage in one arm, the dog pulling her away down the street. Duncan takes a modest bunch, about a dozen stems, holds them slightly away from his body like he's not entirely sure what to do with them.

"For Moira," he says, by way of explanation, which is more than Duncan usually offers.

I take the rest. It's an impractical quantity. I've got my trolley to manage too, and I have to make three trips back and forth from the pile to the trolley basket, and by the time I'm done I've still got deliveries that are now running twenty minutes late.

I push the trolley back to the bakery and I fill every container I can find — the tall plastic jugs, the two jam jars on the windowsill, a ceramic pitcher that usually holds wooden spoons, a spare bread tin with some water in the bottom. I run out of containers and put the last few stems in the big stock pot with a bit of water.

The first customer of the morning is Alison Breck from the chandler's, who comes in for her usual two rolls at seven sharp. She looks around at the bakery — roses everywhere, on every surface, cold and vivid and filling the room with that smell — and she says: "What's happened in here?"

"Henderson's gate blew open," I say, which is not quite true, but near enough.
