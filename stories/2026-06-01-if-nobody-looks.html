If Nobody Looks

A flinty market florist and the soap-seller who keeps leaving him coffee discover they have each spent a long time being invisible.

Published 7:34 AM SGT·~5 min read

❀

"You're doing it wrong, you know."

Reuben didn't look up from the bucket of dahlias he was stripping. Six in the morning, the market still hung with mist and half the stalls dark, and already someone had opinions. "I've been doing it wrong for thirty-one years," he said. "Be a shame to stop now."

"You're crushing the stems. When you grip them like that." The voice belonged to a young woman in a mustard coat, holding a cardboard tray of coffee she clearly hadn't bought for him. She nodded at his fist. "They drink less. They wilt by Thursday."

"They wilt by Thursday because people don't change their water."

"Sure. That too." She set one of the coffees on his table, beside the honesty box, like it was the most natural thing in the world. "I'm Nadia. I took over the soap stall. The one that used to be cheese."

"I know what it used to be." He'd watched the cheese man pack up in November, watched the daughter clear the last of it in a single grim afternoon. He hadn't said anything then either. That was the thing about the market nobody told you when you started: you learned everyone's leaving by the empty trestle where they used to be.

Nadia didn't go away. She stood there sipping her coffee while the mist burned off, watching him bunch the dahlias—coral and rust and a deep bruised purple—with a frankness that should have annoyed him more than it did.

"You always frown like that?" she asked.

"Only when observed."

She came back the next Saturday. And the one after.

Always with the second coffee, always with some small correction delivered in that bright unbothered way, as if she hadn't noticed he was a closed door with the bolt thrown. Your sign's faded. You could group by colour, people buy more when it's grouped by colour. You ever think about doing weddings? He answered in grunts and the occasional dry shot that, to his irritation, made her laugh out loud—a real laugh, head back, startling the pigeons.

He did not ask her questions. That was his policy, with everyone, for a long time now. You asked questions and people thought you'd signed up to carry the answers.

But he noticed things, because noticing was different from asking. He noticed she arrived earlier each week. He noticed the soap stall, three down on the left, didn't have many customers. He noticed she lingered longest at his table on the cold mornings, warming her hands on a coffee she drank slowly to make last, and that some Saturdays the brightness looked like something she put on the same way she put on the mustard coat—deliberately, against the weather.

"You're not eating that," he said one morning, nodding at the pastry going soft and untouched by her elbow.

"I'm savouring it."

"You've savoured it for an hour."

She looked at the pastry. Something crossed her face, quick, and was tidied away. "Money's a bit—the stall's not. People don't need soap the way they need cheese, turns out." She smiled, full wattage. "I'll figure it out. I'm good at figuring things out."

Reuben said nothing. But when she turned to greet a passing customer he took the fattest bunch of dahlias, the coral ones, the ones he'd have got eight pounds for, and he wrapped them in brown paper and he put them in her tray when she wasn't looking, the way she'd been putting coffees on his table for two months.

She found them when she got back to her stall. He watched from a distance—pretended not to, watched anyway.

She stood very still. She looked at the flowers a long time. Then she looked down the row, straight at him, across the heads of the Saturday crowd, and she didn't smile. Her face did something more complicated and more honest than smiling, something that made his chest go tight and unfamiliar.

She walked back over. Set the dahlias on his table between them.

"Why," she said.

Not a question, quite. Reuben picked up a stem, examined it like it might explain him. "You said it yourself. I crush them. These were going to wilt by Thursday." A pause. "Seemed a waste to let nobody see them first."

"Reuben."

"You drink your coffee too slow," he said, gruffly, "to fool anybody who's paying attention. And I've been paying attention." He cleared his throat, looked out at the stalls, the mist long gone now, the whole market loud and golden and alive. "Thirty-one years I've stood here. People come, people go, I watch the empty tables. I'd got comfortable being one of the things nobody looks at twice." He pushed the flowers an inch toward her. "Then somebody did."

For once, Nadia had nothing bright to say. Her eyes were wet. She picked up the dahlias and held them against the mustard coat, coral on yellow, and she was—he let himself think the word—luminous.

"They group well," she said thickly. "By colour. Told you."

"You did."

"I'm right about the stems too."

"Don't push it."

She laughed, that real one, head back, and the pigeons scattered, and Reuben Asante found that he was smiling in front of witnesses, and could not for the life of him be bothered to stop.

She started bringing two coffees and a pastry she actually ate. He started saving her the best of the buckets. By spring the soap stall had a bucket of dahlias on the corner—free, a sign in his blocky hand: TAKE ONE. THEY WILT IF NOBODY LOOKS. And every Saturday, in the thin grey six o'clock light, two people who had each spent a long time being invisible made absolutely certain the other one was seen.
