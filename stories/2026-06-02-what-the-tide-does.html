What the Tide Does

Eight years after leaving the small Irish town she grew up in, Maeve returns — and finds that Finn Callahan, and the ache she'd outrun, are exactly where she left them.

Published 7:22 AM SGT·~13 min read

❀

Finn hadn't changed. That was the first problem.

I'd spent eight years in London becoming someone entirely different — sharper, faster, fluent in spreadsheets and planning committee jargon and the particular exhaustion of always being the youngest woman in the room trying to sound like she belonged there. I'd expected to come back to Kilmore Cove and find everything diminished, the way home places go small when you've been away. I'd expected Finn Callahan to have gone thick around the middle, or sad around the eyes, or to have developed one of those hobbies middle-aged men pick up to fill time — woodworking, maybe, or strong opinions about craft beer.

Instead he was standing in the doorway of the Aisling Inn with a tea towel over his shoulder, barefoot on the flagstone, and he smiled at me like I'd been gone twenty minutes.

"Maeve Corrigan," he said. "You look exhausted."

"I just drove four hours from Dublin."

"I know. I made soup."

That was the thing about Finn. He didn't build to anything. He just arrived at the point, warm and certain, and waited for you to catch up.

My father's hip had shattered on a Tuesday, which was exactly the kind of thing my father would do — inconvenient timing, maximum drama, total insistence that he was fine. He was not fine. He was seventy-one and stubborn and living alone in the house I'd grown up in on Strand Road, and my sister Orla was in Vancouver and therefore morally exempt from the emergency return, which left me.

I'd meant to stay at Dad's. I'd planned it out on the motorway — the inflatable mattress, the weeks of logistics, the working-from-home arrangement with my manager that would require a twelve-point email to execute. But Dad's spare room had, in my absence, become a museum to his model railway obsession, and there was genuinely nowhere to sleep except on top of a 1:76 scale recreation of the Galway-to-Westport line.

Every B&B in Kilmore Cove was full. June, tourist season, the kind of coastal weather that makes people from Germany and the Netherlands feel they are having an adventure. There was one room left anywhere in the village.

Finn's room. Finn's inn. Finn, opening the door before I'd even knocked.

"How long?" he asked, carrying my bag before I could argue.

"Two weeks. Maybe three." I followed him up the narrow stairs. The inn smelled of woodsmoke and salt and something baking. "I'll pay the full rate, obviously."

He laughed. Not mockingly — Finn didn't do that — but with genuine amusement, like I'd said something endearing. "Obviously," he agreed, and showed me to a room with a low ceiling and a view of the grey Atlantic and a small vase of sea thrift on the windowsill, and I stood there with my coat still on and felt something I hadn't expected.

Relief. Inconvenient, disorienting, entirely unwelcome relief.

The thing about being efficient is that it works very well until you're somewhere it has no application.

My days in London had structure. I was good at structure. I had a system for emails (flagged, archived, actioned by noon), a system for groceries (Sunday, forty minutes, the Waitrose on Marylebone High Street), a system for the relationship that had ended in February with Marcus saying, in that infuriatingly calm way of his, that I treated us like a project with deliverables. I'd wanted to argue. I'd opened my mouth and then realised I had a timeline on my phone for Valentine's weekend.

At the Aisling Inn, there were no systems. Breakfast appeared when it appeared. The Wi-Fi worked when it felt like it. Biscuit — a large, philosophically inclined golden retriever who had apparently been Finn's mother's dog and then simply refused to leave after she died — occupied whichever chair was warmest and could not be relocated by any force known to humanity.

"You could move him," Finn said, on the second morning, finding me standing in the dining room holding my laptop and looking at Biscuit occupying the only chair near the power socket.

"I tried."

"You have to ask him."

"He's a dog."

"He's Biscuit." Finn crouched down and said something quietly into the dog's ear, and Biscuit sighed — a full, theatrical sigh — and relocated two feet to the left, freeing the chair. Finn looked up at me with entirely unearned satisfaction.

"I hate it here," I said.

He just handed me a mug of tea. Milk, one sugar. The way I'd drunk it since I was sixteen.

He'd been listening to my calls. Not deliberately, I was sure — the inn's walls were old stone, thick but not impermeable, and his office was next to the small sitting room I'd commandeered as a workspace. I heard his calls too: suppliers, a linen company arguing about a minimum order, a local fisherman who provided the kitchen with crab and apparently also required forty minutes of conversation about his nephew's hurling prospects before any business could be transacted. Finn's voice through the wall was low and even and unhurried in a way that I found profoundly irritating.

I was aware this was not his fault.

On the third evening he made dinner — not as a formal inn arrangement but just because I was there, he said, and he'd made too much — and we sat at the kitchen table while the wind came in off the water and Biscuit slept on the tiles, and Finn asked me about London without performing interest. He just asked. And I talked, more than I'd intended, about the promotion I'd been given last year that had pulled me sideways out of the design work I actually loved and into a management role I was competent at but found completely airless.

"Did you take it?" he asked.

"Of course I took it. It was a significant pay increase."

"Right." He turned his wine glass. "But did you want it?"

I didn't answer. He didn't push. That was the other thing about Finn — he had the patience to let a silence mean something, which was a quality I'd spent years trying to engineer out of myself because silence in meetings meant someone else filled it and usually got credit for your idea.

We sat with it for a moment, and the wind leaned into the window, and Biscuit snored.

He found me on the beach on the fourth night.

I hadn't been hiding, exactly. I'd just gone for a walk at ten o'clock because sleep wasn't coming and Dad was stable and I had too many thoughts with nowhere to go. The strand below the inn was empty at that hour, the tide drawing back, the lights of the village a warm smear behind the dunes. I'd walked until my trainers were wet and then stood there being irresponsible about it.

Finn appeared at the edge of the dunes with two jackets — one on, one in his hand, which he held out without comment.

"How did you know I was here?"

"Biscuit tracked you." He nodded at the dog, who was already investigating something in the tide wrack with focused professional attention.

I took the jacket. It smelled like him — woodsmoke, something coastal, a washing powder I remembered from childhood sleepovers at his house. I put it on and didn't say anything about that.

We walked. The Atlantic was doing what it always did, patient and enormous and entirely indifferent to our particular small dramas. I told him about Marcus. Not with self-pity — I was genuinely past it — but because it felt relevant to the larger question I'd been failing to answer since February, which was what I actually wanted from the next ten years. Marcus had said I treated us like a project. My manager had told me, gently, that I was excellent at the strategic work but that I should think about whether I found any of it meaningful. My friend Cass had looked at me across a wine bar table last month and said, "Maeve, do you ever stop running?"

"I don't think I'm running," I said, which came out aloud, directly to Finn, which I hadn't planned.

"No," he agreed, like I'd said something reasonable. "I think you're just not sure what standing still would feel like."

I looked at him.

"You always had to be going somewhere," he said. "Even when we were kids. You'd arrive at a place and immediately start figuring out how to get to the next one."

"That's not a flaw."

"I didn't say it was." He stopped walking. The water was twenty feet away, pulling back, and the wet sand held the last of the light. "I almost left once," he said. "To go to London."

I went very still.

"When you'd been there about a year. I had this idea that I'd — I don't know what I had. I'd go over, figure out the city, be somewhere near you." He said it plainly, the way he said most things, without dressing it up or softening it. "I didn't say anything because I couldn't work out if it was about London or if it was about you, and it seemed important to know which one before I turned up."

"And did you? Work it out?"

"Yeah." He looked out at the water. "Mam got sick the next year, so it was moot. But yeah, I worked it out."

He didn't say which one. I didn't ask. I stood in his jacket on the dark strand with the Atlantic going about its indifferent business, and something old and unnamed rearranged itself quietly in my chest.

We walked back in a silence that felt entirely different from the ones before it.

I booked the flight at half eleven that night.

It was a practical decision. That's what I told myself, sitting on the low bed with my laptop and the sea audible through the single-glazed window. I had a meeting Tuesday. The inflatable mattress situation at Dad's had been resolved — Orla had ordered a fold-out bed online, it would arrive Monday, I wasn't needed. Dad had his neighbour Mrs Hennessy who brought him soup daily and treated his convalescence like a social event. I wasn't abandoning anyone. I wasn't running. I was being efficient.

The flight was seven a.m., which meant leaving by five.

I set the alarm for four, and packed my bag, and got into bed, and lay there watching the curtain move in the draught from the window frame. Outside, the sea did what the sea does. It didn't care about my meeting. It didn't care about my timeline. It just kept at it, the long pull out and the slow return, all night.

At eleven-forty I got up and finished packing properly. Rolled everything neat. Zipped the case.

There. Decided.

At ten past midnight, I was standing in the narrow hallway at the bottom of the stairs with my wheelie bag and my coat on and my handbag over one shoulder, and Biscuit was sitting on my left foot.

He wasn't doing anything dramatic about it. He'd appeared silently from wherever dogs go at night and placed himself there, and was now simply occupying my foot with the full philosophical weight of his existence. I looked down at him. He looked up at me with the expression he had, which suggested he knew something I didn't and was prepared to wait.

"Move," I said.

He did not.

I heard Finn on the stairs before I saw him — a creak on the third step, the one that always went. He came down in a grey t-shirt and old jeans, like he hadn't been asleep either, and he stopped when he saw the bag.

He looked at it for a moment. Then at me.

He didn't ask me why, or where I was going, or what I was thinking. He didn't say don't go, which was what I'd half been braced for, the thing that would have been easy to argue with. He didn't perform hurt. He just stood there at the bottom of his stairs in his dead mother's inn with the sea coming through the walls, and he looked at me the way he always had — like he could see the whole of me and had already decided it was fine.

Then he said: "I'm glad you came back. Even just for this long. I want you to know that."

Six words in the first sentence, five in the second. Nothing asked for. Nothing taken. Just offered, the way he'd offered the jacket and the tea and all the rest of it — with no strings, no expectation of return. Just Finn being Finn, which had always been more than enough and which I had, apparently, spent eight years trying to outrun.

Biscuit was still on my foot.

I put my handbag down on the flagstone. Not a dramatic gesture — I just set it down, and the clunk of it was very loud in the narrow hall.

"The flight's at seven," I said.

"Mm."

"I have a meeting Tuesday."

"Right."

"My dad has a fold-out bed coming Monday."

Finn said nothing. He leaned one shoulder against the wall and looked at me with that unhurried patience that I used to find maddening and now found, at twelve minutes past midnight in a draughty hallway with a dog on my foot, something very close to home.

"I don't know what I'm doing," I said. It came out simply, because I was too tired to make it complicated.

"No," he agreed. "Neither do I."

"That's not — that's not very reassuring."

"I know." Something moved in his expression. "But I'm not in a hurry, Maeve. I'm not going anywhere. I've got a dog and an inn and a fisherman who calls me every Tuesday about his nephew. I've got time." He paused. "I'm just saying. If you wanted to figure it out from here, for a bit — there's room."

The sea shifted against the walls of the building. A long, slow exhale of it.

I looked at my wheelie bag. I thought about the Marylebone Waitrose, and the Sunday system, and the forty-minute flag-archive-action routine, and the management role that paid well and meant nothing, and Marcus's calm voice saying deliverables, and Cass asking when I'd last stopped.

I thought about Finn on a dark strand saying I worked it out.

"I'd need the power socket in the sitting room," I said.

"Biscuit will have opinions."

"I'll manage Biscuit."

Finn smiled. Not wide — just that quiet, certain smile, the one that had been on his face in the doorway when I'd arrived, the one that had apparently been there, patient and undemanding, for a very long time.

He reached out and took the wheelie bag from my hand. Lifted it easily. Turned and started back up the stairs.

"Tea?" he said, over his shoulder.

"It's midnight."

"Is that a no?"

Biscuit relocated himself from my foot to the bottom stair, looked up at me, then turned and padded up after Finn.

I stood in the empty hallway for a moment. The inn settled around me — old stone, old wood, the particular creak and breath of a building that had been standing here a long time and intended to keep at it. Through the wall the Atlantic went on doing its patient, tireless work: pulling back, returning, pulling back, returning.

I didn't know what London looked like from here yet. I didn't know what Tuesday looked like, or next month, or the version of my life that wasn't always already aimed at somewhere else. I didn't know what to call the thing currently assembling itself quietly in my chest, the thing that had been there, I suspected, since long before I'd ever left.

But I picked up my handbag. I followed the dog up the stairs.

Some things, it turns out, don't need a name to begin.
