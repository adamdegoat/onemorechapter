Margin Notes

A teenager on a community-service placement in an antiquarian bookshop brings in the one book whose handwriting he cannot read.

Published 7:35 AM SGT·~5 min read

❀

Tomas wedged the ladder against the poetry shelf and climbed three rungs before Mr. Okonkwo told him, without looking up, that he was doing it wrong.

"The ladder's fine," Tomas said.

"The ladder is fine. You are wrong." The old man turned a page of the ledger he was always bent over, though Tomas had never once seen a customer buy enough to justify a ledger. "You climb to the book. Wrong direction. You let the book come to you."

Tomas, sixteen and on the third day of a community-service placement he hadn't chosen, decided not to point out that books did not, as a rule, fly. He'd been sent to Okonkwo's Secondhand & Antiquarian for "low-level criminal damage"—a smashed bus shelter, a dare, a bad night—and the magistrate had a fondness for what she called restorative placements. Forty hours. He'd counted them in his head like a prison tally.

"What am I even looking for," he said.

"Mrs. Adesanya's order. Tennyson. Green spine, foxed, with a name in the front." Okonkwo finally looked up, and his eyes were sharp behind enormous glasses. "Top shelf. You were right about that. Bring it down without bending the spine and I will not tell the magistrate you sighed at me."

The book had a name in the front, just as he'd said. To Florrie, who reads too fast — slow down, the good bits are in the corners. Your H., 1948.

Tomas stood at the bottom of the ladder and read it twice. "Who's Florrie?"

"Dead, probably. Most of them are." Okonkwo held out his hand for the volume. "But look." He opened it to a poem Tomas couldn't have named, and there in the margin, in faded pencil, someone had written this is the bit he means with a little arrow. "H. was right. She did read too fast. But she went back. You can tell—different pencil, see, sharper. She read it again when she was older. After he'd gone, I'd guess."

"How do you know he'd gone?"

"Because the second note says I slowed down. Too late, but I slowed down." Okonkwo closed the book gently. "People think a secondhand book is worth less because someone's written in it. They are exactly backwards. The writing is the book. The rest is just the paper it came on."

Tomas didn't say anything. But he didn't reach for his phone, either, which for him was the equivalent of a standing ovation.

He came back the next day without being told the hour, which Okonkwo noted and did not mention.

The work was nothing—shelving, dusting, carrying. But the old man had a way of making nothing into something. Every book that passed through Tomas's hands came with an interrogation. Whose was it? Why this underlining? A student, look at the panic in those margins—exam season. And this one, see, never opened past page forty. A gift from someone who didn't know them at all. Tomas started to see it. The bookshop wasn't full of books. It was full of people, pressed flat and waiting, hundreds of them, talking to each other across decades in pencil.

"You could sell these for more," Tomas said, "if you cleaned the writing out. There's rubbers that'd do it. Erasers."

Okonkwo looked at him the way you'd look at someone who suggested improving a cathedral with a coat of magnolia. "Why," he said slowly, "would I murder them?"

On the ninth day, Tomas brought something from home.

He didn't explain it. He just put it on the counter—a paperback, swollen with damp at some point in its life, the cover long gone. His mother's. He'd found it when they cleared her flat, eighteen months ago now, and he'd kept it for reasons he'd never let himself look at directly.

"I can't read her writing," he said. The words came out rougher than he meant. "In the margins. She wrote loads, all through it, and her writing's rubbish and I can't—I wondered if you—" He stopped. "You're good at it. The old writing. Working out what people meant."

Okonkwo did not make it a big thing, which was how Tomas knew it was a big thing. He pulled the lamp closer, settled his enormous glasses, and bent over the swollen pages the same way he bent over everything—like the contents mattered, like the person who'd held the pen was still, in some way, in the room.

It took an hour. Tomas dusted shelves that were already clean.

"Here," Okonkwo said at last. "She's arguing with the book. Disagreeing with the hero—she thinks he's a fool, look, idiot, just TELL her." A dry sound that might have been a laugh. "She was funny, your mother."

"Yeah," Tomas said, from somewhere behind the travel section.

"And here. This one's not to the book." Okonkwo went quiet for a moment. "It says, read this to T. when he's older. The bit about coming home." He looked up over the glasses. "T.," he said. "I would hazard a guess."

Tomas came out from behind the travel section because his legs decided it for him. He took the book. The pencil was faint and the hand was terrible and it was, unmistakably, hers—read this to T.—a note written to a future she hadn't known she'd miss, a margin she'd left for him on purpose.

His forty hours ended on a Friday. He didn't mention it. Neither did Okonkwo.

On Saturday Tomas turned up at the usual time, wedged the ladder against the poetry shelf, and climbed two rungs before he stopped, and waited, and let the silence do what the old man had taught him silence was for.

"Wrong direction," Okonkwo said, not looking up from his ledger.

Tomas climbed down. He picked up a pencil from the counter—Okonkwo kept a jar of them, sharp—and he opened his mother's book to the bit about coming home, and under her terrible faded hand he wrote, in his own, I read it. I'm slowing down.

"The good bits," he said, "are in the corners. Right?"

For the first time in forty-one days, Okonkwo smiled with his whole face.

"Now," he said, "you are doing it correctly."
