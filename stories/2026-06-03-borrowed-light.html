Borrowed Light

Two guests exiled to the back table of a wedding reception find that being slightly lost is something you can have in common.

Published 7:21 AM SGT·~4 min read

❀

A white taper candle, barely the length of her thumb. Nadia isn't sure why she slipped it into her clutch — only that when the gust came through the propped-open terrace door and took out the centrepiece, something in her refused to let the light just go.

She's sitting at Table Overflow, which is what she has privately named the back table where they've put the guests who don't quite assemble into any obvious social unit. There's a retired couple who seem to have come to the wrong wedding and are handling it with immense dignity. There's a woman in a sequined blazer who keeps refreshing her phone. And there is, across the mismatched arrangement of chairs, a man she hasn't met, who has just watched her take the candle out of her clutch and hold it in both hands like she's warming herself on it.

"You know that's not lit," he says.

"I'm aware."

"Just checking."

He has an easy way of sitting, like he has no particular argument with the chair. Dark jacket, loosened tie. When he looks at her, he actually looks — not the flickering-eyes social scan, but something more settled.

"Rafael," he says, and reaches a hand across the table.

"Nadia." She shakes it. "I worked with Sophie in uni. Architecture school."

"I worked with Priya at her last job. Events planning." He glances at the candle. "So you're a hoarder of small things."

"I prefer collector of ambient mood."

He smiles at that — not a performance of a smile, just the real kind that reaches his eyes a half-second after his mouth. From the next room comes the opening bars of the first dance, something slow and brass-heavy, and the sound makes the air in here feel more private by contrast.

The retired couple takes this as their cue and wanders toward the dancing. The sequined blazer woman gets a call and steps outside. Nadia and Rafael are, more or less, alone with the unlit candle and the white tablecloth and the remains of a bread roll neither of them finished.

"Do you know anyone else here?" she asks.

"Not really. You?"

"Technically yes. Practically — " She makes a small gesture at their table, at the absence of the others. "I've been a bit hard to be around lately. Not difficult. Just not — present. My friends have been patient about it."

She doesn't know why she said that. It came out instead of the usual oh, I know a few people, I've been so busy. She looks down at the candle.

"Work?" he asks.

"I haven't stopped in eight months. I keep thinking the project will end and I'll remember how to be a person, but there's always another project."

"And tonight?"

"Tonight I put on a dress and told myself it counted."

He's quiet for a moment, but it doesn't feel like a gap to fill. "I moved cities eighteen months ago," he says. "For a relationship. It ended six months in. I've spent the last year trying to figure out if I moved to London for her or for me, and I still don't entirely know."

"Do you need to?"

He looks at her, slightly surprised. "Probably not."

"It sounds like you're here. That's something."

"You're here," he says, and there's no weight on it, no invitation — just an observation, mild and precise. You're here. And she realises she is, genuinely here, for the first time in a while — not logged on, not behind glass, but sitting at a back table at Sophie's wedding holding a useless candle and actually feeling the warmth of the room on her arms.

The song in the next room shifts into something with more rhythm. Someone laughs — a big full laugh, the kind that makes strangers smile. Nadia and Rafael both turn toward the doorway at the same moment, and then back at the same moment, and find they're closer than they'd calculated.

She should probably say something easy. He doesn't move.

"Can I ask you something strange?" she says.

"Go on."

"When you noticed things were good again — not fixed, just good — was there a moment? Or did you just look up one day and realise it had happened without you watching?"

He doesn't rush the answer. The candle sits between her palms. Outside the terrace door, a breeze moves through but this time doesn't reach them.

"There was a morning," he says. "I made coffee and I stood at the window and I watched a man trying to park an enormously impractical car in an enormously small space for — I'm not exaggerating — eleven minutes. And I was rooting for him. And I thought, oh. I'm back."

Nadia laughs, and it comes from somewhere real, lower than the polite version she's been deploying all evening. "Did he make it?"

"He did not."

"But you were back."

"But I was back."

She sets the candle down on the table, carefully, like it might matter where it lands. The brass in the next room builds. She thinks about eight months, and the project that never ends, and the dress she put on and told herself it counted, and this table with its mismatched chairs and the bread roll nobody finished.

"I don't actually need to be back in there," she says. "If you wanted to stay here a bit longer."

Rafael leans back in his chair, easy and unhurried, and looks at her with that settled attention.

"I wasn't planning on going anywhere," he says.
