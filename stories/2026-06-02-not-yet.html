Not Yet

A rooftop garden, a begonia against all odds, and a Saturday morning in which Jae realizes that hope, said aloud, changes everything.

Published 7:22 AM SGT·~5 min read

❀

Six months ago, Clara Adesanya murdered a begonia. She'd bought it from the market stall on the corner, carried it up seven flights because the lift was broken, and planted it in the wrong soil with too much water in the wrong corner of the rooftop garden, where it would spend most of the day in shadow. Jae-won Park watched this happen and said nothing, because he'd only moved in three weeks earlier and didn't yet know how to talk to her without his thoughts going sideways.

The begonia had bloomed this morning. First time. Pink-orange, wide open, unreasonably beautiful given its history.

Jae is standing beside it when Clara pushes through the rooftop door at half past eight, a reusable cup in each hand and flour on her sleeve from something he'll never ask about. She stops when she sees him. She always stops, just for a second, like she's recalibrating. He's never been sure what to do with that.

"I brought you one," she says, holding up the right-hand cup. "Oat flat white. I guessed."

"You didn't guess," he says. "You've made that order for me three times."

"I retain information." She crosses the gravel toward him, and her eyes go to the pot before they go anywhere else. "Oh," she says. "Oh, Jae."

He watches her crouch down. She sets both cups on the raised bed beside her and puts two fingers very lightly on the edge of one petal, the way you'd touch something you were afraid of waking.

"It's actually alive," she says.

"It is."

"I did everything wrong."

"Nearly everything," he agrees, and she laughs, and the laugh opens up her whole face, and he has to look at the begonia.

She stands, picks up her cup, wraps both hands around it even though it's already warm outside, the kind of London summer morning that won't last but is trying its best. The city is quiet below them. Pigeons. A siren, far off. The raised beds are full of courgettes and sweet peas and a rosemary bush that's been there since before either of them arrived.

"I came up early because I wanted to tell you something," Clara says. She's looking at the begonia, not at him. "I'm leaving. Next Saturday. I took that job in Bristol."

"I know," Jae says.

She turns. "You know?"

"Mrs. Okafor in 4B told me. She was very upset about it."

"She told me she was fine with it."

"She lied."

Clara is quiet a moment. "I kept thinking I'd tell you up here. Every Saturday. And then I'd come and the tomatoes would need tying in, or the courgettes would have gone completely feral overnight, and I'd think — next week." She looks at him. "You were going to give me the pot today, weren't you. Whether I was leaving or not."

He'd set it on the edge of the highest raised bed, where it would catch the morning light. He'd cleaned the pot. He doesn't confirm or deny this.

"Did you know it would bloom today?" she asks.

"I hoped," he says.

The word settles between them like something placed there with care. Clara's thumb moves on the side of her cup. A plane crosses overhead, low and slow, heading west.

She has been in this garden with him for six months of Saturdays. She's seen him crouched in the dirt at seven in the morning, arguing quietly with a leek. She's seen him carefully label every seedling in a handwriting so small she once borrowed his reading glasses as a joke and then felt something shift in her chest when he'd handed them over without a word. She'd told herself that what she felt on Saturday mornings was simply the garden — the early light, the satisfaction of small green things, the particular peace of working beside someone who didn't need to fill the silence.

She'd told herself that right up until she found the flat in Bristol and felt, underneath the excitement, a specific weight she couldn't account for.

"Jae," she says.

"I know," he says. "You're going."

She looks at him. He's watching the begonia with the focused attention he gives to things he's pretending not to feel strongly about. She knows this about him. She's learned it, Saturday by Saturday, without meaning to.

"I haven't signed the lease yet," she says.

He's very still.

"I haven't signed it," she says again, not because he didn't hear her the first time, but because she's hearing it herself. "The deadline's Tuesday. I keep opening the email and closing it again."

Jae picks up his flat white. He takes one sip. He sets it back down. And then he turns to look at her properly, which he doesn't do often enough, or maybe does too often — she can never decide which.

"There are things I should have said six months ago," he says. "I had a lot of reasons not to."

"Were any of them good reasons?"

He considers this with the same seriousness he brings to everything. "At the time," he says. "Not now."

The morning holds. Somewhere below, a window opens. The sweet peas nod in a small warm movement of air and then are still again.

"Tell me one," she says. "One thing you should have said."

He looks at her for a long moment. Then he looks at the begonia — pink-orange and wide open, alive against all reasonable expectation.

"Stay for breakfast," he says. "And then stay."

Clara Adesanya sets down her cup. She does not say yes yet. She does not say anything yet. She looks at Jae-won Park in the early summer light on the roof of the building where they both, for now, live, and she thinks about an email she has not yet opened, and a lease she has not yet signed, and a begonia that survived six months of being loved the wrong way before it finally bloomed.

She reaches out and straightens the pot so it catches the sun full on.
