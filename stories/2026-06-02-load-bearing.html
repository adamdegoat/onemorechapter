Load-Bearing

When Pita volunteers to teach digital literacy at a public library, she expects to help — she doesn't expect to find the part of herself she thought she'd lost for good.

Published 7:22 AM SGT·~12 min read

❀

Pita stands at the front of the room with her clicker in one hand and fourteen slides she now understands are wrong. She knew it the moment she walked in — the room is smaller than she imagined, the projector screen slightly crooked, and the five people seated at the tables are looking at her with the particular stillness of people who have decided to be polite before anything has been said. The silence has weight. She can feel it pressing on her sternum the way a bad load calculation presses on a beam — quietly, then all at once.

She clicks to slide one. Welcome to Digital Literacy: Your Journey Begins!

The woman in the front row raises her hand.

She hasn't said anything yet. Pita hasn't said anything yet. She raises her hand anyway, and the gesture is so precise, so composed, that Pita has the disorienting feeling that she is the student and this is the correction she has already earned.

"Yes," Pita says.

"I can't read that from here," the woman says, "and I'm not sure I want to."

Her name, Pita learns, is Amara Ofei. She states it the way she states everything — clearly, without decoration. She is seventy-five, Ghanaian-British, and she spent thirty-one years teaching primary school in Lewisham, and nothing in this room frightens her. She simply wants to know: what does the thing actually do, and why.

The others introduce themselves while Pita is still recalibrating. There is Mr. Hartmann — Werner, though he doesn't insist — who is seventy-four, barrel-chested, with a white moustache and the careful suspicion of a man who has been sold things before and regrets most of them. He drove from Hammersmith. He wants to email his son. His son lives in Hamburg. This is the whole of his stated ambition and it is enough. There is Yusra, sixty-two, Somali-British, who already owns a smartphone and uses it confidently for calls and nothing else, and who sat down and immediately read the posted instructions on the wall twice. There is a couple from Stoke Newington — Lin and Frank, both retired, both arriving separately and apparently surprised to see each other, and now sitting at opposite ends of the same table with the charged neutrality of people mid-disagreement about something they haven't discussed yet.

Pita looks at her fourteen slides. Slide four is titled The Cloud: A Helpful Metaphor! and features an image she found on a stock photo site of actual clouds above a mountain range, which she now realizes communicates nothing to anyone about anything.

"Okay," she says.

She pulls the clicker cable from the laptop and closes it.

This is the first competent thing she has done all morning. She can feel it — a small, specific rightness, the way the right tool feels different from the wrong one in your hand. She pulls a chair from the stack against the wall and drags it to the nearest table and sits down, and everyone shifts slightly, reconfiguring, and just like that the room changes its mind about what it's going to be.

"What do you actually want to learn?" she says.

There is a pause.

Then Yusra says: "Photos. My daughter has a baby. She sends me videos and I cannot make them bigger and I miss his face."

And then they're all talking.

The library around them is alive in the usual Saturday way — children in the picture book section performing increasingly ambitious sound effects, a printer somewhere printing something with great urgency, two teenagers lying on the floor of the graphic novel aisle in a posture of complete abandonment. A kid with a volunteer badge who looks about seventeen is troubleshooting something at the self-checkout, his lanyard swinging as he leans in. He glances over at Pita's group, and she catches his eye, and he gives her a thumbs-up that is either genuine encouragement or mild pity. Possibly both.

She starts with the phone. Yusra's phone, because Yusra has one and because Yusra holds it with the confidence of someone who is ready to be useful. She shows her how to expand a video with two fingers. It takes twice. The second time, Yusra's grandson's face fills the screen, round and startled-looking, and Yusra makes a sound that is not a word at all — just warmth, released.

"Can you do that again?" Lin asks.

Yusra shows her. Just like that.

Mr. Hartmann is not interested in videos. Mr. Hartmann has a tablet, which he produces from a canvas bag with the same energy a man might produce a document he has been asked to sign under duress. He places it on the table and regards it. "My nephew gave me this," he says. "I don't know what he was thinking."

"Does it turn on?" Pita asks.

"Of course it turns on," he says, as though this were obvious. It doesn't, immediately. Then it does.

Amara, through all of this, is watching Pita. Not unkindly. Just taking notes — the way a teacher does, cataloguing, categorizing. She has a tablet too, newer than Mr. Hartmann's, and her granddaughter has set it up for her. She can navigate the home screen. What she cannot understand — what nobody has explained to her satisfaction — is where anything goes when she puts it somewhere. Where it lives.

"The Cloud," she says, reading from the handout Pita printed. Which Pita now notices is the summary of the slides she has just abandoned. "This is yours?"

"That's mine," Pita confirms.

"It doesn't make sense to me," Amara says. "A cloud is a thing in the sky. My photographs are not in the sky. So where are they?"

And here is where something shifts in Pita, quietly and without ceremony, the way things shift in a structure when the load is redistributed correctly — not a change in the material, just a change in where the weight falls.

She thinks: where are they.

She thinks: okay. Let me tell you about load distribution.

"It's not a cloud," Pita says. "That word is wrong for it. Here's what it actually is."

She borrows a notepad from the library desk — the teenager, Obi, his badge says, gets it for her without being asked — and she draws the shape of a server room: banks of machines, a building somewhere in a city, temperature-controlled, redundant power supplies, the whole architecture of a place designed to hold things safely so they don't need to be held anywhere else. She draws the path from Amara's tablet to the wifi router to the internet to the server and back again. She draws it the way she'd draw a load diagram — forces and vectors, every element connected to every other element, the whole thing legible if you understand which parts bear weight and which parts merely transmit it.

"Your photograph doesn't move," she says. "It copies. One version stays in your tablet. One version goes to the server and lives there. And the server is designed to keep things alive even if one part fails, because it's built in layers — the same way a bridge has redundant cables so that if one snaps, the structure holds. You never see the cables. But they're there."

Amara is very still for a moment.

"So the server is the cable," she says.

"The server is the cable," Pita says.

"Good," Amara says, and makes a note.

Something eases in Pita's chest. Not everything. But something. She has not thought like this in two years — not felt her own mind do this particular thing, this reaching for underlying structure, this translation of the invisible into the legible. She thought she'd broken it. She thought the thing that made her useful was a casualty of what happened with the Meridian project — the calculations she'd signed off on, the subcontractor's corner-cutting that nobody caught until the settlement inspection, the forty-seven revision cycles and the night she sat in a car park in the rain and decided she was no longer someone who should be trusted with anything load-bearing.

She is standing in a public library explaining server infrastructure to a retired primary school teacher, and she feels, for the first time in approximately twenty-six months, like herself.

The word for this is not relief. The word for this is something she doesn't have yet. She lets it sit unnamed and gets on with it.

By eleven o'clock the room has reorganized itself without Pita organizing it. This is the thing she notices — she is not teaching so much as moving through a space where teaching is occurring, a distributed system, load-sharing, redundant and resilient. Yusra is showing Frank how to zoom. Lin has discovered the library's app and is investigating it with the focused energy of someone who suspects it contains something she needs. Mr. Hartmann has his email open — Obi helped with the password — and is composing a message to his son in Hamburg with two fingers and an expression of absolute concentration, the same expression Pita imagines he wore when he was reducing a sauce.

Amara has found the camera on her tablet and is taking photographs of the table, the window, her own hand, Pita's notepad diagram. She reviews each one and then takes another. She is running an experiment.

"Is it there?" she asks, pointing at her photo roll. "On the server?"

"Only if you've turned on backup," Pita says. She checks. She has. "Yes. It's there."

"Even if I drop this on the bus and it breaks."

"Even then."

Amara places the tablet down with the quiet satisfaction of someone who has just understood something that was worth understanding. "Good," she says again.

She looks at Pita for a moment. "What did you do before this? Your job."

"I was a structural engineer," Pita says.

"Structures."

"Bridges, mostly."

A pause. Amara picks up the tablet and scrolls through her photographs again. "And then?"

"And then I stopped."

Amara doesn't ask why. This is the thing about her — she doesn't fish. She waits, and if you want to tell her, you tell her, and if you don't, she moves on. Pita doesn't tell her. Amara nods, once.

"Bridges fall down sometimes," she says. Not gently. Not as comfort. Just the way she'd state a fact to a ten-year-old who already knows it and needed someone to say it aloud. "Good engineers know which ones to keep and which to let go."

She returns to her photographs.

Pita doesn't say anything. There is nothing to say to a true thing.

She takes the bus home in the early afternoon, her laptop bag on her lap, the printed handouts still inside it and largely unused. The city moves past the windows in the particular way Saturday cities move — loose, a little sideways, under no particular obligation to be anywhere soon. She feels strange and not unpleasantly so.

Her flat is on the fourth floor of a building that is fine. She has lived here for nineteen months since the move from Wellington — the job that brought her here evaporated when she couldn't bring herself to take the projects they offered, and she's been existing since then on consulting fees from things she can do remotely with her eyes closed, things that ask nothing of the part of her that used to matter.

The notebooks are on the shelf in the second bedroom, which she calls her office and uses mostly for storage. She's known they were there the entire time. She's not pretended they aren't.

She goes in.

They are exactly where she left them — seven of them, spiral-bound, the covers soft with handling. She picks one up. Her own handwriting, the load calculations for the Penrose footbridge, the numbers she could still run in her sleep. She puts it back. She picks up another, more recent — the Meridian project notes, thicker, the later pages denser with annotation, and she doesn't open this one because she knows what's inside and she has looked at it enough times already in the dark at 3 a.m. to have memorized its shape.

She sets it back on the shelf.

She sits down at her desk. Opens her laptop. The screen blooms to life. Her email client is there — she never closed it — and she looks at it for a moment: the inbox, the unread messages from two years ago she still hasn't archived, the name of her old colleague Marcus Reill in the list, the email he sent eight months after she left Wellington, which she read fourteen times and never answered.

She closes the laptop.

She makes tea.

She drinks the tea standing at the kitchen window watching the street.

She goes back to the desk and opens the laptop again.

The cursor blinks in the compose window. She types Marcus's email address. She types: Hi Marcus — I've been thinking. I think I'm ready to look at something small. If there's anything around. She reads it back. She hits send before the revision instinct can pull it back.

Then she sits very still with her hands in her lap while her heart does its irregular, inconvenient thing.

She doesn't know what he'll say. She doesn't know if he'll say anything at all. She doesn't know what something small looks like yet, or whether she can hold it. She knows that she sat in a library today and felt her own mind do the thing it does, and she knows that mattered, and she knows that two lines sent to a person who once trusted her is not a bridge — not even close — but it is a cable, laid. One element of what will eventually be a system. The structure doesn't arrive all at once. It assembles. Load by load, connection by connection, until one day the thing holds.

She closes the laptop more gently this time.

The following Saturday, Amara is there before Pita.

She is sitting at the same table, her tablet in front of her, her bag on the chair beside her. She has been there for a while — there is already a paper cup on the table from the library café, half-empty. She looks up when Pita comes in.

"I practiced," she says. "On my phone. Ten times."

Pita sets down her bag. She didn't bring the slides. She doesn't need the slides. "Show me," she says, and sits down.

Amara demonstrates. She opens her photos, expands one using two fingers, navigates to her backup status, finds the wifi settings unprompted. It is smooth and confident. It is also slightly wrong in one step — she's found a route that works but the long way, adding two taps she doesn't need.

Pita shows her the shorter path.

Amara does it again. Then again. She's clocked it now — Pita can see the moment of registration, the recalibration, the way true understanding looks different from memorization. Her head lifts slightly. She looks at Pita with the expression of someone who has just fit a piece into a structure and felt it click flush.

"Next week," Amara says, with complete seriousness, "I will be faster than you."

Pita laughs. Full, real, not managed — the kind of laugh that comes from a place below decision, the kind she'd forgotten she still had access to. It goes up into the high library ceiling, travels through the sound of the children's section and the self-checkout and the printer, fills the small room the way air fills a room when someone opens a window and you suddenly understand the room had been slightly airless for a while.

She thinks: oh.

She thinks: there it is.
