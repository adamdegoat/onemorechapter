Clarendon Street, 7am

Blessing thinks she's organized the whole street party herself. By sundown, she discovers she's been part of a community all along — she just needed to put up a sign.

Published 7:22 AM SGT·~5 min read

❀

Blessing Nwosu has organized the whole of Clarendon Street's summer party by herself. That, at least, is what she tells herself at 7am on a Saturday, dragging a wheeled suitcase full of bunting along a pavement that is still cool in the shade.

The suitcase is heavy. She packed it the night before with the kind of grim efficiency she uses for quarterly stock audits: bunting in a ziplock, cable ties counted out into a sandwich bag, the laminated permit from Lambeth Council folded to the exact size of her back pocket. She has a list on her phone. She has a backup list in her notebook. She did not sleep past 5am.

She had signed up on an impulse. The email had her street's name in the subject line and she'd opened it before she'd fully woken up, and then she'd clicked the volunteer form before she could think clearly, and then she'd been the only person at the planning meeting in February, sitting across from a council liaison named Derek who seemed pleased and faintly puzzled. "Brilliant," Derek had said, and given her a folder.

She has been carrying that folder for four months. She got the permit. She designed the flyer in Canva, using a font she now finds slightly embarrassing. She ordered four hundred metres of red-and-yellow bunting from a wholesaler in Coventry. She called the council again when they lost her application. She called them again.

And now she is on Clarendon Street at 7am on the 31st of May with six folding tables rented from a hire company in Stockwell, arriving by van at 8am, and she is the only person here, and the street is quiet, and she is fine. She is absolutely fine.

She starts with the bunting. She clips the first length to a lamppost, the plastic clip colder than she expected, and walks the line of string to the chestnut tree on the opposite pavement, and she is measuring the sag — there's a precise sag that looks festive rather than sad — when she hears a door.

Mr. Osei from No. 31 comes out in a green polo shirt, carrying a folding table under each arm. He walks toward her at a pace that suggests he has somewhere specific to be.

"Where do you want these?" he says.

She stares. "Mr. Osei, I haven't — the van isn't here until eight."

"I have four more inside," he says. He is already setting one down on the pavement, snapping the legs out with the efficiency of a person who has done this many times. "From my son's wedding. They've been in the garage."

He doesn't say you're welcome because she hasn't thanked him yet. He goes back inside. She looks at the table. It is a good table. It is better than the rented ones, which she has been worrying about since she booked them.

By 7:45am, Mr. Osei has brought out all four of his tables plus a box of clip-on tablecloth weights because, he explains, there's always a wind off the park. She clips the weights on. They're the right size.

The van arrives early. The two Kapoor boys from No. 22 are suddenly there, seventeen and fifteen, eating cereal out of the box, apparently doing nothing, and then apparently carrying a table each without being asked. Their names are Arjun and Dev. She has lived here for two years and she is only learning this now.

Dev asks about the music. She tells him there's a playlist. He nods and does not unpack his Bluetooth speaker yet, which she respects.

Mrs. Chen from No. 14 comes at half past nine with a pot. The pot is large enough that she's carrying it in both arms with the focused expression of someone transporting something that matters. She sets it on Mr. Osei's best table and lifts the lid and the smell that comes out — star anise, soy, something slow-cooked and sweet — lands on the street like a second summer. The buns inside are perfect, folded-over crescents, and Mrs. Chen arranges them on a platter she has also brought with no apparent expectation of comment.

"My mother's recipe," she says, when Blessing says something inadequate. That is the whole explanation.

The paddling pool appears at the far end at ten o'clock. The Naths from No. 7 drag it out and fill it from a garden hose, their daughter screaming with joy about it before the water is even properly cold. Two other children appear from somewhere within minutes. Then four. Blessing does not know all the families yet but she can see them walking toward the sound, the way people do.

At 2pm she is eating a char siu bao and talking to Mr. Osei about Accra, about the traffic there versus the traffic here, about whether traffic is the same everywhere or just differently terrible, and she does not look at her phone once.

At 4:30pm, when the light is going amber and the Kapoor boys have found a playlist that is somehow acceptable to everyone between the ages of six and seventy-one, Mr. Osei brings her a piece of paper. Handwritten. A list of names and numbers, twenty-seven of them, in different inks, with a header that says: Clarendon Street Residents' Committee – Next Year.

She looks up at him.

"I'm already on it," she says. She doesn't plan to say it. It comes out.

Mr. Osei smiles, the particular smile of someone who knew something before you did. "I know," he says.

She looks back at the list. Twenty-seven names. Twenty-seven people who live on her street, whose doors she has passed a thousand times. She finds her own name near the top, added at some point during the afternoon without her noticing, in handwriting that is not hers.

She holds the paper and looks at Clarendon Street: the tables, the bunting with its precise sag, the paddling pool, the pot, the two brothers arguing pleasantly about the next song. She has been here for two years. The community has been here for longer.

She had just needed to put up a sign.
